## Baseline Output

The COVID origins debate is a strong stress test for epistemic mapping because the same broad evidence base has produced sharply different probabilistic judgments. FLF frames the case as especially difficult: two expert judges in the Rootclaim debate ruled decisively for zoonosis, while multiple Bayesian analyses diverged by enormous amounts. Scott Alexander's review portrays the debate as an unusually detailed public test of explicit Bayesian reasoning, with Rootclaim favoring lab leak and Peter Miller arguing successfully against it.

The sources in this slice show why a simple bottom line is risky. Rootclaim concedes that Miller won the debate, but maintains that lab leak remains most likely and criticizes the live format and the judges' probabilistic inference. Good Judgment's superforecasters favored natural zoonosis in the aggregate, but not unanimously; a persistent minority judged a biomedical research-related accident most likely. This means the aggregate probability and the distribution of disagreement should both be preserved.

The technical disagreements also divide into narrower subquestions. Market geography can support a zoonotic reading, but its meaning depends on whether the market is treated as an origin site, a superspreading location, or a place affected by ascertainment and infection-location assumptions. Debarre and Worobey reject Weissman's proximity-bias inference, while Levin's Bayesian working paper reports a very large lab-leak-favoring odds ratio using a four-part decomposition. Weissman's later critique of the multiple-introduction argument may weaken one zoonotic subclaim, but it does not by itself resolve the entire origins question.

Overall, this slice should be represented as a structured disagreement rather than as a verdict. A careful synthesis should distinguish adjudicated debate outcome, participant postmortem, aggregate forecasting, minority disagreement, formal Bayesian decomposition, geography-bias critique, and later phylogenetic subarguments. The most important open questions are whether the market-location evidence survives bias critiques, whether Bayesian decompositions are robust to correlated assumptions, and what future evidence would move the assessment.
