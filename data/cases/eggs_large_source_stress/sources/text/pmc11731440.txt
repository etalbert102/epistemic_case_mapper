# Association between egg consumption and risk of obesity: A comprehensive review

## Abstract

Eggs serve as a vital source of high-quality protein and lipids in the human diet, contributing significantly to nutritional intake; however, the relation between egg intake and health risks has been controversial. This study aimed to assess the relationship between egg intake and obesity and the effects of the various nutrients in eggs on obesity were separately investigated. This review involved searching Scopus, PubMed, Google Scholar for relevant articles from 2002 to 2022. Studies suggested that moderate egg consumption exerts little effect on blood lipid levels, that due to the body regulates endogenous cholesterol production in response to the external cholesterol intake. Furthermore, certain studies also verified that the presence of other nutrients in eggs, such as lecithin, unsaturated fatty acids, and apolipoproteins, not only does not contribute to elevated blood lipids but also plays a role in regulating lipid metabolism to prevent obesity. Additionally, the study reveals that different cooking methods significantly impact the nutritional composition of eggs, with soft-boiled eggs generally being the most advantageous for human health. This article reveals that dietary cholesterol or moderate egg intake was not significantly associated with a higher risk of obesity in healthy adults. Nevertheless, cholesterol-sensitive individuals should ensure moderate cholesterol intake.


## Introduction


In terms of sources of high-quality protein for the human body, meat, eggs, and milk are at the forefront. As a whole-nutrient food, eggs need a very wide range of people; almost everyone needs to eat nutritious food every day. Chinese people consume ∼400 billion eggs annually, with the per capita egg consumption of China being in the top three globally. According to the analysis, 100 g of egg contains 12.58 g of protein, mainly in the form of egg albumin and globulin, which contain eight types of amino acids that the human body needs. The composition of human protein is very similar to that of egg protein, and the human body's absorption rate of egg protein can be as high as 98 %. Every 100 g of egg contains 11–15 g of fat, which is mainly concentrated in the egg yolk. This fat can be easily digested and absorbed by the human body. Egg yolk is rich in lecithin, sterols, calcium, phosphorus, iron, and vitamins A, D, and B ( Alexander et al., 2016 ). In addition, eggs are a rich source of essential nutrients as well as a source of dietary cholesterol; for example, one large egg contains ∼186 mg of cholesterol. Although there is no direct evidence indicating that egg consumption increases cholesterol levels, the American Heart Association's (AHA) 2000 Revised Dietary Guidelines recommend the consumption of <300 mg of cholesterol per day to minimize elevated blood cholesterol levels ( Krauss et al., 2000 ).

Presently, the global prevalence of obesity continues to increase. Nearly one-third of the global population is overweight or obese. In America, 31.9 % of adults are affected by obesity, of which 13.7 million are <19 years old ( Paixao et al., 2023 ). Obesity is also prevalent in China. Approximately 600 million Chinese people, that is, 43.6 % of the population, are overweight or obese ( Wang et al., 2021 ). Obesity has become a massive problem in the world, and the harm of obesity to health has been garnering increasing attention. The World Health Organization (WHO) defines obesity as an abnormal or excessive accumulation of fat that has an adverse effect on health. Clinically, the diagnosis of obesity is based on body mass index (BMI), which is calculated as weight divided by the square of height (kg/m 2 ) and shows a good correlation with body fat percentage. When choosing a BMI cutoff to define overweight/obesity, the most important consideration is the increased risk of complications. The BMI cutoff points for overweight and obese adults are 25 and 30 kg/m 2 , respectively, because of the increased health risks observed at these indices ( Bauer et al., 2020 ). For adults, a BMI of 25.0–29.9 kg/m 2 is defined as overweight and a BMI of ≥30 kg/m 2 is defined as obese ( Yue et al., 2024 ). Clinically closely related lipids mainly include cholesterol and triglycerides. Elevated plasma cholesterol and triglyceride levels are closely associated with the development of atherosclerosis, and these two are also key items in the measurement of blood lipids. Fifty-five meta-analysis studies have shown that higher blood cholesterol level is associated with a high-cholesterol diet intake. Cholesterol as a nutrient intake of the human body. A Chinese study reported that high dietary cholesterol intake is associated with hypercholesterolemia, and if the cholesterol content is high, it can easily affect blood lipid levels ( Huang et al., 2019 ). Blood lipids are the general name of cholesterol, triglycerides, and other lipids in serum. The clinically closely related blood lipids mainly include cholesterol and triglyceride, and the plasma cholesterol and triglyceride levels are key items in the measurement of blood lipids.

As the main lipid in eggs, although high cholesterol intake can lead to obesity, the relationship between egg consumption and obesity is currently inconclusive. Presently, most research on eggs and health is based on the premise that cholesterol intake from eggs can increase blood cholesterol levels; however, to the best of our knowledge, there is currently no relevant research to prove this ( Richard et al., 2017 ). Evidence from epidemiological studies supports the conclusion that the consumption of one to two eggs daily does not affect blood cholesterol levels in healthy people who consume a careful diet. A survey of egg consumption in China revealed that adults who consumed three to six eggs weekly exhibited the lowest risk of various diseases. However, those who consumed <1 egg weekly demonstrated an increased risk of cardiovascular disease and overall mortality by 22 % and 29 %, respectively, whereas those who consumed ≥10 weekly exhibited a 39 % and 13 % increased risk, respectively ( Kalogeropoulos and Papanastasiou, 2019 ). Presently, there are numerous studies on egg consumption and human health, including cardiovascular disease, diabetes, blood pressure, and lipids; however, studies regarding the relation between egg consumption and obesity are insufficient and more data are required to prove this relation ( Guo et al., 2018 ; Park et al., 2018 ).

This study aimed to explore the relation between egg consumption and obesity and to examine the relationship between egg consumption and obesity incidence by considering potential confounders including overall diet quality. With sufficient research regarding the relation between egg consumption and obesity, we hope that more precise dietary guidelines regarding egg intake can be developed.


## Methodolgy


The aim of this study was summarizing and comparing the relationship between the egg intake and the incidence of obesity, and discussing the effects of different components of eggs on lipid metabolism. Conduct a bibliometric analysis by searching keywords regarding the egg consumption, obesity, hyperlipidemia, BMI, cholesterol, fatty acids, phosphatidylcholine. The keyword was implemented in the web of Scopus, PubMed, Google Scholar for relevant articles from 2002 to 2022. We excluded Short Communication, Case Report, Proceedings Paper and incomplete data. This review only considered information published in English.


## Obesity


In 1998, the National Institutes of Health recognized obesity as a disease with negative effects on individual health and socioeconomic costs ( Hales et al., 2017 ). The prevalence of obesity in the United States was 39.8 % in 2015–2016 and is projected to reach 48.9 % by 2030( Ward et al., 2019 ). With the rapid development of the social economy, the lifestyle and diet structure of domestic residents have undergone considerable changes, and the prevalence of Chinese residents who are overweight or obese has also shown a substantial upward trend. Since 1990, the prevalence of overweight and obesity in Chinese adults has increased by an average of 1 % annually.

Visceral fat is mainly present in the abdominal cavity and is distributed around vital organs such as the stomach, liver, and intestines. The human body requires a certain amount of visceral fat to support and protect the internal organs; however, excessive visceral fat is very harmful to health. Visceral adipose tissue is biologically active, such as in endocrine organs, producing hormones that can affect health (disrupting the normal hormonal balance) and other adipokine (passing through the portal vein and entering and harming the liver). It also increases insulin resistance and the risk of serious diseases such as cardiovascular disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, breast cancer, and colorectal cancer. Presently, obesity has become a major public health problem that endangers health.

## Global egg consumption


Historical records show that with increase in the income level of residents of a country, the diet structure enhances, mainly manifesting in a substantial reduction of the direct consumption of grains and increase in the consumption of livestock and poultry products. The global diet has been constantly changing, and the coronavirus disease (COVID-19) pandemic has made the changes even more drastic. Consumers are seeking low-cost but nutritious food, and eggs are becoming increasingly popular worldwide owing to their protein content and numerous other nutritional benefits.

According to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, global egg production is steadily increasing. As one of the countries with the highest egg consumption globally, the United States Department of Agriculture data show that per capita egg consumption in the United States in 2020 reached 290( Drouin-Chartier et al., 2020 ). Based on a report published on 2020, the annual per capita egg consumption in Japan increased from 325 to 337 eggs over the past decade, an increase of 3.5 %( Kang et al., 2022 ). In 2019, the per capita consumption of eggs in Germany was 236( Reithmayer and Musshoff, 2019 ). China is the world's largest producer and consumer of eggs, and its egg production has ranked first in the world since 1985. According to the International Egg commission (IEC), in 2020, China's per capita egg consumption was 298, ranking fifth worldwide ( Liu et al., 2021 ) ( Fig. 1 ). ## Egg substabces be associated with obesity


Eggs are a type of food with special nutrition that can be easily digested and provide most of the nutrients required for human growth and health maintenance. It contains essential nutrients such as lipids, proteins, vitamins, minerals, trace elements, long-chain omega-3 fatty acids, lysozyme, lecithin, and other bioactive substances. In addition, it provides moderate energy (∼140 kcal/100 g), with high cooking potential and low economic cost. Every 100 g of egg yolk contains 328 kcal, 15.2 g of protein, 1510 mg of cholesterol, 112 mg of calcium, 438 μg of vitamin A, and 630 mg of lecithin ( Fig. 2 ). Eggs have been identified as the lowest-cost animal source of protein, vitamin A, iron, vitamin B12, riboflavin, and choline and the second lowest–cost source of zinc and calcium ( Drewnowski, 2010 ). The increase in fat in eggs is mainly dependent on the increase in the ratio of yolk to egg white, where the total lipid content is relatively stable, ranging from 8.7 to 11.2 per 100 g of whole eggs. Yolk lipids comprise 65 % neutral lipids, 30 % phospholipids (PLs), and 4 % cholesterol. Lipids are part of the vitelline lipoprotein whose structure comprises a core of triglycerides and cholesterol esters surrounded by a monolayer of PLs and cholesterol, wherein apolipoproteins are embedded. Phospholipids are the most important value-added component of egg lipids, comprising 78 % phosphatidyl choline, 17 % phosphatidylethanolamine, 2.5 % sphingomyelin, 0.5 % phosphatidylinositol, phosphatidylserine, and lysophosphatidylcholine ( Ali et al., 2017 ). A diet rich in egg PLs considerably enhances endothelial vasodilation and reduces daytime systolic blood pressure in patients with metabolic syndrome ( Skorkowska-Telichowska et al., 2016 ). Among the various components of eggs, cholesterol, lecithin, and fatty acids may be associated with obesity.


## Cholesterol


Eggs are one of the richest sources of cholesterol in the diet. The yolk of an egg contains ∼2.7g of cholesterol per 100g. The high cholesterol content in eggs contributed to the decrease in egg consumption 30–40 years ago, although numerous studies conducted in the 1990s reported a lack of correlation between egg consumption and high plasma cholesterol levels. Some recent studies have also highlighted the beneficial effects of eggs on humans, and some authors have also shown that egg cholesterol is not fully absorbed ( Lopez et al., 2017 ; Kim and Campbell, 2018 ). As the most common and traditional food, eggs, owing to their cholesterol content, were once thought to have an adverse effect on plasma cholesterol levels, a dogma that has not been supported by recent research findings. A meta-analysis has shown that both low-density lipoprotein (LDL) and HDL increase in healthy individuals after consuming eggs. However, LDL: HDL, as a cardiovascular disease risk marker, does not increase disease risk when its ratio is the minimum increase value ( Rouhani et al., 2018 ). Therefore, an increase in high-density cholesterol may weaken the impact of an increase in low-density cholesterol, and other components in eggs may also have beneficial effects ( Miranda et al., 2015 ). Wells and Bronte Stewart et al. reported that increasing the amount of dietary cholesterol intake tends to have relatively small effects on serum cholesterol levels. When cholesterol intake was ∼500 mg per day, the data appeared to follow a square root relation, but higher intakes did not lead to any further increase in serum cholesterol levels. Not everyone responds the same way to dietary cholesterol intake, as this response is highly variable and depends on genetic and metabolic factors ( Knopp et al., 2003 ; Herron et al., 2006 ). Most people (two-thirds) experience no or only a slight increase in serum cholesterol levels when consuming large amounts of dietary cholesterol. These individuals are classified as low responders or compensators because they can compensate by reducing cholesterol biosynthesis, absorption, and excretion ( Katan and Beynen, 1987 ). Conversely, a small subset of the population has a considerably larger increase in serum cholesterol levels (100 mg of dietary cholesterol leads to an increase in serum cholesterol of ≥2.3 mg/dL)—these people are classified as high responders or noncompensators.

There are two main sources of human blood cholesterol: endogenous and exogenous. Endogenous cholesterol refer to the components synthesized in the human liver, fat, and other tissue cells, while exogenous cholesterol refer to the one obtained from food. Under normal circumstances, exogenous and endogenous cholesterol restrict each other and jointly maintain the balance of human lipid metabolism. The conversion rate of exogenous cholesterol to serum cholesterol is low, and there are individual differences in cholesterol absorption. Egg cholesterol intake does not considerably affect blood lipid cholesterol levels, and normal egg intake does not cause dyslipidemia, which leads to metabolic obesity. Thus, it cannot be proven that normal intake of egg cholesterol causes obesity in humans.


## Lecithin


PLs are amphiphilic lipids present in all plant and animal cell membranes, arranged as lipid bilayers. The PLs present in most cell membranes are basically glycerophospholipids (GPLs). GPLs extracted from food products (e.g., soybeans, egg yolk, milk, or marine organisms such as fish, roe, or krill) are defined as “dietary GPLs”. Dietary supplementation of PLs can treat inflammatory diseases, regulate inflammatory response, and play an important role in inhibiting tumor and metastasis. Studies have found that PLs can also regulate blood lipids, reduce the risk of cardiovascular disease, and regulate neurodevelopment and immune function ( Tse and Byrne, 2020 ; Tsimikas and Witztum, 2024 ; Zhang et al., 2024 ). Currently, PLs are widely employed to treat liver diseases, including viral hepatitis and alcohol-induced liver damage. Egg yolk PLs are important contributors to the overall dietary PL intake. Among them, 10 % of the total PLs in egg yolk is lecithin ( Zhao et al., 2022 ).

Eggs are an important source of PLs in the diet. Numerous studies have highlighted the hydrophilic and lipophilic properties of yolk lecithin's molecular structure, which actively participate in the emulsification reaction. Consequently, this facilitates the emulsification process of cholesterol and neutral fat accumulated within blood vessels while concurrently reducing blood viscosity. Additionally, a daily intake of 1.2 mmol/L of yolk lecithin inhibits cholesterol absorption and transport and prevents obesity ( Yang et al., 2018 ). Egg yolk–derived PLs reportedly reduce the intestinal absorption of cholesterol and triglycerides in rats in a dose-dependent manner ( Blesso, 2015 ). Fang Yang et al. believed that PLs in egg yolk play a regulatory role in cholesterol absorption, and a diet rich in eggs can substantially reduce cholesterol absorption, inhibit de facto cholesterol synthesis and deposition in the form of cholesterol esters, activate bile acid synthesis in liver, and promote bile acid excretion in feces ( Yang et al., 2012 ).


## Fatty acids


Excessive dietary fat intake is an important factor that causes the accumulation of obese fat. Eggs, as a cholesterol-rich food, are usually rich in animal protein and saturated fatty acids (SFAs)( Marshall, 2019 ). Recent studies have shown that the occurrence of obesity not only depends on the amount of dietary fat but also on the type and composition of fatty acids, especially the type and composition of unsaturated fatty acids. The relative amounts of unsaturated (monounsaturated fatty acids, MUFA + polyunsaturated fatty acids, PUFA) to saturated fatty acids in egg yolks (5.31 g vs 2.64 g/100 g whole eggs) are particularly high compared with those of other foods of animal origin. In food fats, monounsaturated fatty acids are oleic acid and polyunsaturated fatty acids are linoleic acid, linolenic acid, arachidonic acid, and so on. Egg yolks are also a rich source of linoleic acid (FA 18:29c, 12c [n-6]). Chang et al. found in a dietary trial of healthy people that if the PUFA + MUFA/SFA ratio is <3, changes in the PUFA/SFA ratio (0.5, 1, or 2) do not affect the levels of plasma total cholesterol, lipoprotein cholesterol, and TAG ( Chang and Huang, 1990 ). Increases in plasma VLDL-C and VLDL-TAG are associated with increased levels of MUFA in the diet. High dietary MUFA levels also lead to increased liver cholesterol accumulation. It is not yet possible to predict the appropriate food supply P + M/S ratio or P/S ratio, but a P + M/S ratio of dietary fats <3 can be considered. Studies have shown that dietary myristic acid (C14:0) and linoleic acid (C18:2) are the main fatty acids that regulate plasma cholesterol—C14:0 is the most dominant saturated fatty acid that raises plasma cholesterol, and C18:2 is the only fatty acid that consistently lowers plasma cholesterol ( Harlioglu et al., 2012 ; Samson et al., 2020 ). In eggs, the C14:0 (myric acid, 0.024 g/100 g whole egg) concentration is much lower than the unsaturated fatty acid C18:2 (linoleic acid, 1.38 g/100 g whole egg).

Unsaturated fatty acids can regulate lipid metabolism, thus inhibiting the process of fat growth, causing the body to consume a large amount of energy, avoiding fat accumulation, and accelerating the growth and development of muscle protein through the optimization of protein dynamics, and their epigenetic correction is mostly achieved through the regulation of miRNA levels, and they ultimately play a role in the regulation of metabolic syndrome. The specific mechanism still needs further study. Studies have shown that unsaturated fatty acids cannot be ignored in the process of reducing body fat percentage and promoting the improvement of metabolic efficiency ( Machate et al., 2020 ). According to the mechanism of action, unsaturated fatty acids can effectively regulate the expression level of intestinal flora and adipocyte-controlled genes, thus promoting the change of adipocytokines or interfering with their corresponding mediated signaling pathways to a certain extent, inhibiting individual appetite and affecting various metabolic rates such as lipid metabolism, energy metabolism, and muscle anabolic metabolism ( Silva et al., 2017 ; Miyamoto et al., 2019 ).

These data confirm that eggs are not directly associated with the incidence of hyperlipidemia in healthy patients. Nevertheless, people who have a high response to dietary cholesterol (approximately 15 %–25 % of the population) must control their egg intake, because increased egg intake in these people has a greater effect on blood lipids than people with a low response.


## Protein


Egg whites and yolks are rich in protein. The protein concentration averaged 12.5 g per 100 g of raw eggs, while the yolk and its yolk membrane and the egg white contained 15.9 g and 10.90 g of protein per 100 g, respectively. Hundreds of different proteins have been identified in eggs that are associated with specific physiological functions to meet specific time requirements during embryonic development. The yolk is a complex environment containing 68 % LDL, 16 % HDL, 10 % live and other soluble proteins, and 4 % photosensitive proteins. Apolipoprotein B, protein-carrying A1, yolk protein, serum albumin, immunoglobulin, ovalbumin, and oval transferrin are the most abundant proteins in the yolk, accounting for >80 % of the total protein in the yolk. Egg white is a gelatinous structure that lacks lipids and mainly comprises water (∼88 %), fibrous structural proteins (ovalomucin), glycoproteins (ovalbumin and protease inhibitors), antibacterial proteins (lysozyme), and peptides. A total of 150 different proteins have been identified in egg whites, and ovalbumin is known to make up ∼50 % of the total protein in egg whites ( Huang et al., 2022 ).

Many studies have reported that apolipoprotein A1 (ApoA1) has anti-obesity effects, and it has been observed that changing circulating ApoA1 levels in mice alters the amount of body fat. For example, mice injected with ApoA1 mimics exhibited rapid weight loss and reduced adipose tissue mass ( Kawachi et al., 2013 ). The main mechanism by which ApoA1 regulates body fat is by driving adipocyte lipolysis. Numerous studies have reported that mice lacking ApoA1 gain more weight and fat mass ( Wei et al., 2014 ). Additionally, ApoA1 activates lecithin–cholesterol acyltransferase and recognizes HDL receptors. Studies using QSG-7701 hepatocytes overexpressing ApoA1 have shown that ApoA1 expression can reduce steatosis by reducing lipid storage and altering lipid transport in hepatocytes ( Cooke et al., 2018 ). The current study also suggests that ApoA1 regulates lipid metabolism by independently stimulating the classical cAMP signaling pathway to increase the lipolysis activity of adipocytes ( Ma et al., 2014 ). The mechanism by which ApoA1 inhibits inflammation also plays an important role in fighting against obesity. Sung et al. employed a homeostasis model assessment approach to assess insulin resistance and found that the Apo B concentrations were significantly higher and that the ApoA1/ApoB ratios were significantly lower during the study period in individuals with metabolic syndromes such as abdominal obesity and hyperlipidemia ( Sung and Hwang, 2005 ). Overall, reduced ApoB secretion leads to reduced CM and VLDL production and increased TG levels in the body, which in turn promotes the progression of obesity.A nested case–control study conducted by Pan et al., a research team at Peking University, found that moderate intake of eggs (approximately one egg per day) was associated with higher levels of beneficial proteins and HDL cholesterol in the blood, especially APOA1 levels. There is less harmful protein and LDL cholesterol, which has a protective effect on the cardiovascular system. All in all, moderate intake of eggs will not only not cause obesity in healthy people but also inhibit obesity to a certain extent.


## Vitamin


Egg yolks, a vitamin-rich food, contain all the vitamins except vitamin C (ascorbic acid). Egg yolks contain high amounts of vitamins A, D, E, K, B1, B2, B5, B6, B9, and B12, while egg whites contain high amounts of vitamins B2, B3, and B5 and also high amounts of vitamins B1, B6, B8, B9, and B12. Eating two eggs a day can meet 10 %–30 % of human vitamin needs. Notably, the content of fat-soluble vitamins (vitamins A, D, E, and K) in egg yolks is highly dependent on the hen's diet.


## Minerals and trace elements


Eggs are rich in phosphorus, calcium, and potassium and contain moderate amounts of sodium (142 mg per 100 g of whole eggs). It also contains all essential trace elements, including copper, iron, magnesium, manganese, selenium, and zinc, with egg yolks being the main source of iron and zinc supplies. The concentration of some of these trace elements (selenium and iodine) depends in part on the dietary diet of the hens.


## Egg consumption and obesity


As a food with a comprehensive nutritional composition, eggs contain various beneficial ingredients to the human body. However, in recent years, there have been many articles on the impact of egg consumption on cardiovascular disease, fatty liver disease, diabetes, and other related indicators ( Shi et al., 2011 ; Shin et al., 2017 ; Wang et al., 2021 ). One of the most important ingredients that affect these related diseases is cholesterol, which is the most controversial aspect of egg consumption. Eggs are one of the most common sources of cholesterol in our daily lives and we must learn more about them. The body itself regulates the production of endogenous cholesterol based on the amount of external cholesterol consumed in the diet, and cholesterol has little effect on blood lipids; hence, the consumption of eggs in moderation will not lead to obesity or being overweight. Herein, from the data collected ( Table 3 ), no significant relation was observed between egg intake and obesity. According to the results shown in the scatter plot ( Fig. 3 ), we can find that when healthy people consume <10 eggs weekly, the BMI value stabilizes within a certain range. Therefore, healthy people's moderate intake of eggs will not lead to obesity. When the weekly intake is between seven and eight, people with a normal weight BMI of <25 are not at risk of being overweight or obese and are maximizing their nutrient intake. Eggs can provide a complete source of protein and contain all essential amino acids as well as vitamins and minerals ( Song and Kerver, 2000 ). As a daily food, eggs provide high-quality protein, trace elements, and antibacterial and antioxidant substances. The yolk protein, Ovo transferrin, and ovalbumin in eggs can inhibit lipid oxidation by clearing free radicals ( Mine, 2007 ). Moreover, eggs contain active lipid components such as unsaturated fatty acids, PLs, choline, carotenoids, and omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids, which are beneficial for overall health ( Shinn et al., 2016 ). However, the high cholesterol content in eggs makes them a food of concern, which has led to numerous studies over the past few decades investigating egg consumption and cardiovascular disease risk; however, there is not much research on the relation between egg consumption and obesity.

The Dietary Guidelines for Chinese Residents emphasize “balanced nutrition” and “food diversity,” with clear recommendations for eggs, offal, and fatty foods. It is recommended to eat 40–50 g (approximately the amount of one egg) of eggs per day and not discard the yolk. Of course, dietary guidelines apply to most people. People who already have hypercholesterolemia, especially those with atherosclerosis, must strictly control the intake of high-cholesterol foods in their diet (the Guidelines for the Prevention and Treatment of Dyslipidemia recommend a cholesterol intake of <300 mg per day); as one egg contains ∼220 mg of cholesterol, such individuals should consume eat no more than one egg a day. Additionally, everyone should make it a habit to have regular medical check-ups every year. It is recommended that healthy people over the age of 20 years get a blood lipid test every 5 years, and people over the age of 45 years should get a blood sugar test every year. In healthy adults, dietary cholesterol or egg intake was not significantly associated with a higher risk of obesity. However, those who are sensitive to cholesterol should still intake cholesterol in moderation.

Owing to their cholesterol content, eggs have become a controversial food among nutrition experts and health institutions. Some researchers believe that dietary guidelines for studying specific nutrients should consider the impact of food sources on health ( Astrup et al., 2019 ). It is difficult to study foods alone without considering their impact on the overall diet and the nutrients they collectively provide. In Western populations, eggs are often eaten with meats that are high in saturated fat (often processed), while in Asian cultures, eggs are often eaten with vegetables ( Xia et al., 2020 ). Compared with people from Asian cultures, Western populations may also consume more cholesterol from other sources (such as red meat, full-fat dairy products, and nonessential foods) ( Su et al., 2015 ; Xu et al., 2018 ; Carson et al., 2020 ). For example, in the American diet, cholesterol and saturated fat increase at the same time owing to dietary patterns, such as eggs with bacon and sausage. Furthermore, different cooking methods can lead to changes in the nutritional composition of eggs. Eggs must be stored at room temperature or in the refrigerator before use. In relatively fresh and hard-boiled eggs, no clear evidence of mineral or vitamin denaturation was observed. The levels of polyunsaturated fatty acids, selenium, and vitamin A seem to decrease when cooked, especially in hard-boiled eggs ( Ramalho et al., 2006 ). Moreover, studies have shown that cooking significantly reduces the oxygen free radical scavenging capacity (antioxidant potential) of the yolk associated with free aromatic amino acids, lutein, and zeaxanthin and also affects the lipid content of the yolk ( Nimalaratne et al., 2016 ; Juhaimi et al., 2017 ). These observations confirm that the way food and eggs are cooked is crucial for assessing the digestibility of eggs and their associated nutritional quality.

In summary, assessing the effects of how eggs are cooked on human health is difficult because many factors are involved. The recommendation to retain most of the nutrients associated with eggs is to consume boiled or soft-boiled eggs, where the egg whites are boiled (to inactivate antinutrient factors and potentially pathogenic bacteria) and the yolk is essentially raw (to preserve most vitamins, lipids, micronutrients, and some bioactive molecules).


## Conclusion


In this comprehensive review, we summarize egg components that may be associated with obesity and discuss their possible effects on obesity separately. These results show that healthy people eat less than 10 eggs per week, the BMI value will stable within a certain range. However, it is important to note that people who have a high reaction to cholesterol should ensure that their egg intake is moderately reduced. Additional nutrients present in eggs, such as lecithin and unsaturated fatty acids, exert a favorable impact on the regulation of human adipose tissue metabolism. To sum up, moderate egg consumption (7 to 8 eggs per week) in healthy people does not lead to obesity or overweight, and dietary egg consumption is not significantly associated with obesity risk.
