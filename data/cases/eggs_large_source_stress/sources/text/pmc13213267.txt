# The Risk of Incident Diabetes Mellitus in Relation to Egg Consumption among Working-Aged Korean Adults

## Abstract

Purpose Conflicting results have been published for the association between egg consumption and the risk of diabetes mellitus (DM). The present study was to assess the risk of incident DM in relation to egg consumption. Materials and Methods Study participants were 91005 non-diabetic adults who periodically received health check-ups. They were categorized into six groups by egg consumption (<1/week, ≥1 and <3/week, ≥3 and <7/week, ≥1 and <2/day, ≥2 and <3/day, ≥3/day) and followed for incident DM during median 6.9 years. The Cox proportional hazard model was used to longitudinally evaluate the hazard ratio (HR) and 95% confidence interval (CI) for incident DM according to the six groups of egg consumption [adjusted HR (95% CI)]. Subgroup analysis was conducted by sex and age (<45 years or ≥45 years). The risk of DM conferred by a daily increment of one egg consumption was also analyzed. Results In all participants, egg consumption ≥1/week was not significantly associated with an increased risk of DM, compared with the reference [<1/week: reference, ≥1 and <3/week: 1.01 (0.91–1.12), ≥3 and <7/week: 0.96 (0.87–1.06), ≥1 and <2/day: 1.06 (0.95–1.19), ≥2 and <3/day: 1.04 (0.88–1.22), ≥3/day: 1.29 (0.98–1.70)]. This pattern of relationship was similarly observed across all subgroups. A daily increment of one egg consumption showed only a marginal association with the risk of DM in men [1.07 (1.02–1.13)] and younger individuals [1.07 (1.02–1.13)]. Conclusion Egg consumption appears to have little association with the risk of incident DM.


## INTRODUCTION


Diabetes mellitus (DM) is an incurable and chronic disease entailing substantial burden on the healthcare system by serious diabetic complications, high economic cost, and reduced quality of life. 1 2 DM was the eighth leading cause of death and disability, affecting nearly 460 million people globally in 2019. 3 A recent report from the International Diabetes Federation announced that the number of DM patients were over 536 million (aged 20–79 years) in 2021 and was expected to reach 783.2 million by 2045. 4 Rapid increase in the prevalence of DM leads to an urgent need for risk assessment and disease prevention for DM.

It has been proven that nutritional intake and dietary pattern are closely linked to the risk of DM. 5 6 In particular, there has been increasing interest in the degree to which commonly consumed food is associated with the risk of incident DM. Egg is an energy-dense and nutrient-rich food that is widely consumed across all age groups within the global food system. 7 The nutritional features of egg have aroused concern about whether high consumption of egg elevates the risk of DM. However, previous studies have published inconsistent results regarding the effect of egg consumption on the risk of DM. 8 9 10 11 12 13 These results included positive 8 9 10 and null 11 12 associations between egg consumption and the risk of DM. It seems that the effect of egg consumption on DM differs by ethnicities, region, and dietary pattern. 13 14 15

Moreover, in a meta-analysis of 19 prospective cohort studies, higher egg consumption was associated with an 8% reduced risk of metabolic syndrome that has a close relationship with DM. 16 These heterogeneous results warrant the need for an additional study to investigate the risk of DM in relation to egg consumption.

In the present study, we quantified the risk of incident DM according to egg consumption among 91005 Korean men and women. In addition, we analyzed how much the increase in daily one egg consumption contributed to the risk of DM. To evaluate the independent effect of egg consumption on the incidence of DM, our adjusted covariates included factors involved in the development of DM. These factors were age, sex, physical activity, body mass index (BMI), smoking, and dietary factors. 17 18


## MATERIALS AND METHODS



## Study design, participants, and exclusion criteria


This retrospective cohort study was conducted by analyzing data from the Kangbuk Samsung Health Study (KSHS). The KSHS was designed to explore the health conditions and medical status of Korean employees and their families who underwent medical examinations at Kangbuk Samsung Hospital. Initial enrollment of study participants was from 162909 participants who completed the Food Frequency Questionnaire (FFQ) as part of a health check-up between March 2011 and December 2012. Their medical records of health check-ups were used as baseline clinical characteristics of the study. For participants who attended multiple health check-ups between March 2011 and December 2012, the first visit was regarded as a reference point. Among initially enrolled study participants, we excluded 54969 participants who were relevant to the exclusion criteria as follows: one or more missing values for an independent variable (egg consumption) and adjusted covariates including body weight, HbA1c, marital status, and alcohol intake (n=42357); history of serious medical diseases such as stroke, coronary heart disease, and malignancy (n=3930); abnormal total calorie intake less than 500 kcal/day or more than 4000 kcal/day (n=4131); and presence of underlying DM (n=4551). Out of the remaining 107940 participants, 91005 participants had received health check-ups at Kangbuk Samsung Hospital between January 2013 and December 2019. Thus, the total number of eligible study participants was 91005.


## Clinical, biochemical, and demographic data collection


In the KSHS study, participants are asked to complete a questionnaire that inquires about their medical history (e.g., previous diagnosis of DM), use of medications (e.g., currently taking anti-diabetic medication), and health-related lifestyle habits (e.g., smoking and exercise). Additionally, questions about age, sex, and marital status are included in a questionnaire. Since a large proportion of the participants were married, study participants were divided into married and unmarried groups. The levels of physical activity were assessed using the Korean-validated version of the International Physical Activity Questionnaire short form ( http://www.ipaq.ki.se ) and were categorized into low, middle, and high levels.

BMI was determined by dividing weight in kilograms by height in meters squared (m 2 ). Obesity was defined as BMI ≥25 kg/m 2 , following the guidelines of the International Obesity Task Force. 19 Blood samples were collected from the antecubital vein after fasting for >12 hours. Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol levels were determined using a homogeneous enzymatic colorimetric test (Advia 1650 Autoanalyzer; Bayer Diagnostics, Leverkusen, Germany). Fasting glucose was measured using the hexokinase method and hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c) was measured using an immunoturbidimetric assay with a Cobra Integra 800 automatic analyzer (Roche Diagnostics, Basel, Switzerland).

DM was identified by reviewing health check-up data according to any of the following criteria: previous diagnosis of DM, currently taking anti-diabetic medication, fasting glucose level ≥126 mg/Dl, or HbA1c ≥6.5%. 20 The presence of underlying DM and new onset of DM were determined by reviewing data from baseline and follow-up health check-up, respectively.


## Nutrition data collection


Nutrient intake of study participants was assessed using the Korean version of the FFQ. This version of the FFQ was specifically developed to semi-quantitatively evaluate the dietary intake of Korean adults. 21 The questionnaire consisted of 106 food items that were commonly consumed by the Korean population. The validity and reproducibility of this FFQ were previously evaluated in a study for 124 Korean subjects. 22 In that study, Ahn, et al. 22 concluded that the FFQ appeared to be an acceptable tool for assessing nutrient intakes in Koreans.

The questionnaire for egg consumption included questions on frequency and amount. For example, the questions about frequency of egg consumption are as follows. “During the past year, how often did you consume eggs?” Replies consist of nine items including never or rarely, once a month, two or three times a month, once or twice a week, three or four times a week, five or six times a week, once a day, twice a day, and three times a day. In addition, the amount of egg consumption at one eating occasion was assessed by the following questions. “How many eggs do you usually eat at one time?” Answers consist of three items with half (25 g), one (50 g), and one-and-a-half (75 g). To enhance the comprehension of participants, photographs depicting the amount of egg consumption were included. Egg consumption was calculated in grams per day and then converted to the number of servings per week or per day.

Total energy and nutrient intake, including dietary fiber intake, were calculated using Can-Pro 3.0 software, which was developed by the Korean Nutrition Society. 23


## Ethical approval


The Institutional Review Board (IRB) of Kangbuk Samsung Hospital approved the study’s methods and participants (IRB No. KBSMC 2022-08-041). The IRB also approved a waiver of informed consent for the study, as it involved analyzing retrospective data with personal information that had been de-identified from routine health check-ups.


## Statistical analysis


Participants were divided into six groups based on their weekly or daily egg consumption: <1 serving per week; ≥1 and <3 servings per week; ≥ 3 and <7 servings per week; ≥1 and <2 servings per day; ≥2 and <3 servings per day; and ≥3 servings per day. The initial clinical parameters are presented as mean±SD for continuous variables and as proportions for categorical variables. Key baseline clinical, demographic, and other characteristics were compared among the six egg consumption groups, with differences evaluated using analysis of variance or Kruskal–Wallis H tests, depending on the data distribution. Differences in categorical variables were examined using the chi-squared test.

To evaluate the incidental risk of DM, the Cox proportional hazards model was employed. Both unadjusted and multivariable-adjusted hazard ratios (HRs), along with 95% confidence intervals (CI), were calculated for the six different egg consumption groups, using the group with the lowest egg intake (<1 serving per week) as the reference. The covariates adjusted in the multivariate model were selected from the established risk factors for DM that were available in our data of health check-up. These factors were age, sex, physical activity, BMI, smoking, alcohol consumption (g/day), hypertension, marital status, LDL-cholesterol, and nutritional factors including total energy intake, dietary fiber intake, and the intake of the three macronutrients (carbohydrates, protein, and fat). The variance inflation factor (VIF) was examined to ensure there was no multicollinearity among the variables, confirming that no variable had a VIF greater than 10. The proportional hazards assumption was checked using log-log plots. For each group, the incidence of cases and incidence density (incidence cases per 1000 person-years) of DM were calculated. The median follow-up period was calculated as the median of participants’ person-years. Person-years were calculated from the date of the first visit to the date of the last visit or the date of DM diagnosis and then converted to years.

We also carried out subgroup analyses based on sex (men and women) and age (≥45 years and <45 years) based on previous research indicating varied outcomes by sex and age. All subgroup analyses utilized the same methodologies and multivariable-adjusted model, except for the sex subgroup analysis, which excluded the sex variable. Furthermore, we examined both unadjusted and multivariable-adjusted HRs with 95% CIs for DM in relation to the daily increase in one egg serving among all participants and within subgroups. Statistical analyses were conducted using R version 4.5.0 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria), and a two-sided p -value of less than 0.05 was deemed statistically significant across all analyses.


## RESULTS


Most of the study participants were working-aged adults, with an average age of 38.8±6.6 years. The baseline clinical characteristics of the study participants are summarized in Table 1 . Compared with the group with the lowest egg consumption (<1 serving per week), the group with the highest egg consumption (≥3 servings per day) presented relatively lower levels in mean age, LDL-cholesterol, and the prevalence of hypertension. The proportion of obesity, total calorie intake, macronutrients (carbohydrates, protein, and fat) intake, and dietary fiber intake increased proportionally to the levels of egg consumption. Over a median follow-up period of 6.9 years, 4895 of 91005 participants were diagnosed with new-onset DM. Although there was no distinct difference in baseline levels of fasting glucose and HbA1c among groups, the incidence of DM was highest in the group with the highest egg consumption.

Table 2 provides a summary of both the unadjusted and multivariable-adjusted HRs and 95% CI for DM in relation to egg consumption. Comparing groups of egg consumption ≥1/week to the group of the lowest consumption (<1/week reference), significant results were not observed in the fully adjusted model [adjusted HRs (95% CI): <1 serving per week (reference), 1.01 (0.91–1.12) for ≥1 and <3 servings per week, 0.96 (0.87–1.06) for ≥3 and <7 servings per week, 1.06 (0.95–1.19) for ≥1 and <2 servings per day, 1.04 (0.88–1.22) for ≥2 and <3 servings per day, 1.29 (0.98–1.70) for ≥3 servings per day]. Subgroup analyses also showed an identical pattern of relationship between egg consumption and the risk of DM. Although group with egg consumption ≥3/day showed a higher value of HR compared to the reference group, statistical significance was not observed due to a widely dispersed 95% CI.

Table 3 presents the risk for DM conferred to increment of one egg serving per day across all participants and subgroups by sex and age. Multivariable-adjusted analysis indicated that an increment of one egg serving per day was marginally associated with the risk of DM in all participants [1.06 (1.02–1.12)], men [1.07 (1.02–1.13)], and the young age group [1.07 (1.02–1.13)].


## DISCUSSION


There has been a long-standing debate whether high egg consumption actually increases the risk of DM. The present study was designed to provide additional evidence for the debate on the association between egg consumption and DM. In a longitudinal analysis of 91005 Korean adults, we found that an increase in egg consumption was not associated with an increased risk of DM in all participants and each subgroup. Although an increment of one egg consumption per day was significantly associated with the risk of DM in men and the young age group, the magnitude of the association was very modest. These findings do not conflict with the guidelines of prominent nutrition organizations, including the American Society for Nutrition, which declared that egg consumption does not significantly harm overall health outcomes in the general population. 24

Previous studies have reported heterogeneous results about associations between egg consumption and the risk of DM. It seems that these discrepancies were largely attributable to differences in geographic region, study design, and population characteristics. When stratified by geographic region and population context, inconsistent findings have been more clearly demonstrated. Several prospective cohort studies conducted in non-US populations have reported findings consistent with our observations. In a Mediterranean cohort of 15956 participants, the highest egg consumption (>4 eggs/week) was not associated with an increased risk of DM compared with the lowest consumption (<1 egg/week), with an adjusted odds ratio of 0.7 (95% CI: 0.3–1.7). 11 Similarly, the Japan Public Health Center-based Prospective Study demonstrated that egg consumption across quartiles was not significantly associated with risk of DM compared with the lowest quartile. 12 Meta-analyses of prospective cohort studies further support this regional heterogeneity, showing that positive associations were observed predominantly in US populations, whereas studies from Europe and Asia generally did not report significant associations. 13 14 15 Moreover, heterogeneous findings have been observed even among the same Asian populations. A longitudinal analysis of 8545 Chinese adults from the China Health and Nutrition Survey reported a dose-dependent increase in DM risk across increasing quartiles of egg consumption. 8 In contrast, another study of 3298 elderly Chinese individuals found no significant association between egg consumption and DM risk across multiple intake categories. 25 Notably, some epidemiologic evidence has suggested a protective association: in a prospective study with 19.3 years of follow-up, egg intake >45 g/day was associated with a 38% lower risk of DM compared with <14 g/day among 2332 middle-aged Finnish men. 26 These findings suggest that differences in study design, number of study participants, age distribution, and follow-up period may substantially modify the association between egg intake and DM.

Results from randomized clinical trials (RCTs) have provided evidence to challenge hypotheses about the potential mechanisms by which high egg consumption might increase the risk of DM. One hypothesis linking high egg consumption to increased risk of DM is the correlation between choline abundant in egg yolk and Trimethylamine N-oxide (TMAO). Choline can be transformed into TMAO by the gut microbiome. 27 TMAO increases oxidative stress and systemic inflammation, which plays an important role in the pathophysiology of DM. 28 Results from cohort studies have reported that individuals with higher baseline TMAO levels have an increased risk of type 2 DM. 29 30 However, it remains unclear whether increased egg consumption actually leads to an increase in TMAO concentration. Despite the potential for choline to be transformed into TMAO, phosphatidylcholine, the major form of choline in egg, did not elevate the TMAO concentration. 27 This finding is further supported by a RCT showing that consumption of 3 eggs/day led to a significant increase in plasma choline levels, but did not affect plasma TMAO concentrations in 23 patients with metabolic syndrome. 31 In addition, consumption of 3 eggs/day led to significant reduction in plasma insulin level and Homeostatic Model Assessment of Insulin Resistance and improvement in status of antioxidants and carotenoids among subjects with metabolic syndrome. 32 33 Another hypothesis has raised concerns that high egg consumption may increase blood cholesterol levels, which in turn can contribute to the development of DM via worsening insulin resistance. 34 However, studies have reported that egg consumption does not raise blood cholesterol levels, 35 and have a beneficial effect on lipoprotein particle profiles. 36

Nonetheless, it should be recognized that not a few studies have reported positive association between egg consumption and the risk of DM. Djoussé, et al. 9 analyzed the adjusted HR and 95% CI for type 2 DM according to egg consumption in a prospective study of 20703 male and 36295 female Americans. Their analysis showed that 5–6 eggs consumption/week [1.46 (1.14–1.86)] and ≥7 eggs consumption/week [1.58 (1.25–2.01)] were significantly associated with the increased risk of DM, compared with no egg consumption. 9 Results from previous meta-analyses also support that high egg consumption is potentially associated with the increased risk of DM. In a meta-analysis of 14 studies with 320778 subjects, the group with the highest egg consumption had a 68% increased risk of DM, compared with the group with the lowest egg consumption. 37 Dose-dependent analysis indicated that each 4/week increment in egg consumption led to 29% increased risk of DM. 37 Two additional meta-analyses also showed that highest egg consumption was associated with 9% and 42% increased risk of DM, compared with lowest egg consumption. 10 38 These results may be attributable to differences in study design and follow-up duration, the specific characteristics of the study populations, the influence of co-consumed foods and overall dietary patterns, and statistical limitations including unmeasured confounding factors. However, it should be recognized that further prospective and well-designed studies should be conducted to establish a clear consensus on the relationship between egg consumption and the risk of DM.

Our study has several limitations. First, most of our study participants are relatively young Korean adults. It has been demonstrated that the effect of egg consumption on DM can vary by region, ethnicity, and age. 13 14 15 Therefore, we acknowledge that our results are less likely to be generalized to other ethnic groups and age groups. Second, we acknowledge that our study is insufficient in the precise assessment of biological and environmental factors that affect the development of DM. In particular, although our analysis was adjusted by several nutritional and behavioral covariates, unmeasured and residual confounding factors could affect the results. These factors include genetic predisposition, family history, the way to cook eggs, co-consumed foods, intake of saturated fatty acids, intake of trans fatty acids, and trauma. Third, the median period of follow-up was 6.9 years, which was not enough to evaluate the long-term risk of incident DM. These limitations warrant further studies with diverse populations, consideration of a wide range of confounding factors, and a longer follow-up period.

In conclusion, our study showed that increased egg consumption was not associated with an increased risk of DM. This finding was similarly observed across subgroups including men, women, younger participants (<45 years), and older participants (≥45 years). In addition, the risk of DM conferred by an increment of one egg serving per day was very modest. These findings support the current notion that increased egg consumption does not independently exert a substantial effect on the risk of DM.
