## Baseline Output

The egg evidence is mixed but points toward a moderate, diet-pattern-based conclusion. Federal dietary guidance and AHA advice no longer use a simple cholesterol-number rule as the main message. Instead, they emphasize healthy dietary patterns that are low in saturated fat, rich in plant foods, and generally keep dietary cholesterol low without compromising nutritional adequacy. The AHA public guidance says healthy people can include up to one whole egg per day, while people with high LDL cholesterol should pay closer attention to reducing both saturated fat and dietary cholesterol.

Observational outcome studies do not give a single answer. The BMJ 2020 cohort studies and updated meta-analysis found that moderate egg consumption, up to one egg per day, was not associated with overall cardiovascular disease risk. It also noted differences by region and possible concerns in people with type 2 diabetes, although the overall conclusion remained reassuring for moderate intake. In contrast, the JAMA 2019 pooled cohort study reported that higher dietary cholesterol and higher egg intake were associated with higher incident CVD and all-cause mortality in a dose-response manner. The egg association became non-significant after adjusting for dietary cholesterol, suggesting dietary cholesterol may explain much of the signal.

Randomized trial evidence adds another layer. Li 2020 found that higher egg consumption in healthy participants increased LDL cholesterol and the LDL-c/HDL-c ratio, though the trials measured biomarkers rather than long-term cardiovascular events and did not establish a clean dose trend. The NNR 2023 scoping review summarized the total picture as follows: RCTs suggest lipid changes with heterogeneous responses, while recent observational evidence does not strongly support harm from moderate intake up to one egg per day, especially in European populations. Overall, eggs can fit within a healthy diet for many people, but the advice should remain conditional on total diet quality, LDL status, diabetes or other risk context, and the foods eggs replace.
