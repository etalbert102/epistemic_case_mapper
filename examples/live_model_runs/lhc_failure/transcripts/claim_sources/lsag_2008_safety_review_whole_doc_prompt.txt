18: 
19: The safety of collisions at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) was studied in
20: 2003 by the LHC Safety Study Group, who concluded that they presented no
21: danger. Here we review their 2003 analysis in light of additional experimental
22: results and theoretical understanding, which enable us to confirm, update and
23: extend the conclusions of the LHC Safety Study Group. The LHC reproduces
24: in the laboratory, under controlled conditions, collisions at centre-of-mass
25: energies less than those reached in the atmosphere by some of the cosmic rays
26: that have been bombarding the Earth for billions of years. We recall the rates
27: for the collisions of cosmic rays with the Earth, Sun, neutron stars, white
28: dwarfs and other astronomical bodies at energies higher than the LHC. The
29: stability of astronomical bodies indicates that such collisions cannot be
30: dangerous. Specifically, we study the possible production at the LHC of
31: hypothetical objects such as vacuum bubbles, magnetic monopoles, microscopic
32: black holes and strangelets, and find no associated risks. Any microscopic black
33: holes produced at the LHC are expected to decay by Hawking radiation before
34: they reach the detector walls. If some microscopic black holes were stable, those
35: produced by cosmic rays would be stopped inside the Earth or other
36: astronomical bodies. The stability of astronomical bodies constrains strongly
37: the possible rate of accretion by any such microscopic black holes, so that they
38: present no conceivable danger. In the case of strangelets, the good agreement of
39: measurements of particle production at RHIC with simple thermodynamic
40: models constrains severely the production of strangelets in heavy-ion collisions
41: at the LHC, which also present no danger.

48: 1 - Introduction
49: 
50: The controlled experimental conditions offered by accelerators permit the detailed
51: study of many natural phenomena occurring in the Universe. Many of the
52: fundamental particles, such as muons, pions and strange particles, were first
53: discovered among the cosmic rays, and were subsequently studied with
54: accelerators. As the energies of accelerators have increased, they have also revealed
55: many heavier and less stable particles, such as those containing heavier quarks, as
56: well as the carrier particles of the weak interactions. Though not present in ordinary
57: stable matter, these particles played important roles in the early history of the
58: Universe, and may still be important today in energetic astronomical bodies such as
59: those producing the cosmic rays.
60: 
61: The energies of accelerators have been increasing regularly over the past decades,
62: though they are still far below those of the most energetic cosmic rays. With each
63: increase in accelerator energy, one may ask whether there could be any risk
64: associated with new phenomena that might be revealed. It has always been
65: reassuring that higher-energy cosmic rays have been bombarding the Earth since its
66: creation with no disastrous side-effects. On the other hand, the particles produced
67: in cosmic-ray collisions typically have different velocities with respect to the Earth
68: from those produced by accelerators, so the circumstances are not directly
69: comparable. The question has therefore been asked whether Earth's immunity to
70: cosmic-ray collisions also applies to accelerator collisions.
71: 
72: The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) accelerator is nearing completion at CERN. It is
73: designed to collide pairs of protons each with energies of 7 TeV (somewhat more
74: than 7000 times the rest mass-energy of the proton), and pairs of lead nuclei each
75: with energies of about 2.8 TeV per proton or neutron (nucleon). Though
76: considerably higher than the energies of previous accelerators, these energies are
77: still far below those of the highest-energy cosmic-ray collisions that are observed
78: regularly on Earth. In light of safety questions about previous accelerators, and in
79: advance of similar questions about the LHC, the CERN management requested a
80: report on the safety of the LHC by the LHC Safety Study Group, a panel of
81: independent experts, which was published in 2003 [1]. This report concluded that
82: there is no basis for any conceivable threat from the LHC.
83: 
84: The advent of LHC operations now revives interest in safety questions, so the
85: CERN management has commissioned us to review the arguments presented in the
86: 2003 report and previous studies of the possible production of new particles, and to
87: update them in light of experimental results from the Brookhaven relativistic
88: heavy-ion collider (RHIC), in particular, as well as of recent theoretical speculations
89: about new phenomena.
90: 
91: We consider all the speculative scenarios for new particles and states of matter that
92: have been discussed in the scientific literature and raise potential safety issues. Our
93: methodology is based on empirical reasoning using experimental observations, and
94: hence could be extended to other exotic phenomena that might be cause for
95: concerns in the future.
96: 
97: We focus our attention mainly on two phenomena of current interest, namely the
98: possible production of microscopic black holes, such as might appear in certain
99: theoretical models featuring additional dimensions of space, and the possible
100: production of ‘strangelets', hypothetical pieces of matter analogous to conventional
101: 
102: 
103: 
104:                                           2
105: 
106: nuclei, but containing also many of the heavier strange quarks. These were both
107: considered carefully in the 2003 report of the LHC Safety Study Group [1]. Their
108: conclusions for strangelets, obtained in connection with heavy-ion collisions, apply
109: with equal or greater force to proton-proton collisions at the LHC.
110: 
111: In the case of microscopic black holes, there has been much theoretical speculation
112: since 2003 about their existence and their possible experimental signatures, as
113: reviewed in [2], where references may be found. In the case of strangelets, detailed
114: experimental measurements at the Brookhaven National Laboratory’s Relativistic
115: Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC) of the production of particles containing different
116: numbers of strange quarks [3] enable one to refine previous arguments that, in the
117: event they exist, strangelets would be less likely to be produced at the LHC than at
118: RHIC.
119: 
120: Before discussing these two hypothetical phenomena in more detail, we first review
121: in Section 2 estimates of the rates of collisions of high-energy cosmic rays with
122: different astronomical bodies, such as the Earth, Sun, and others. We estimate that
123: the Universe is replicating the total number of collisions to be made by the LHC
124: over 1013 times per second, and has already done so some 1031 times since the origin
125: of the Universe. The fact that astronomical bodies withstand cosmic-ray
126: bombardment imposes strong upper limits on many hypothetical sources of
127: danger. In particular, as we discuss in Section 3, neither the creation of vacuum
128: bubbles nor the production of magnetic monopoles at the LHC is a case for concern.
129: 
130: In the case of the hypothetical microscopic black holes, as we discuss in Section 4, if
131: they can be produced in the collisions of elementary particles, they must also be
132: able to decay back into them. Theoretically, it is expected that microscopic black
133: holes would indeed decay via Hawking radiation, which is based on basic physical
134: principles on which there is general consensus. If, nevertheless, some hypothetical
135: microscopic black holes should be stable, we review arguments showing that they
136: would be unable to accrete matter in a manner dangerous for the Earth [2]. If some
137: microscopic black holes were produced by the LHC, they would also have been
138: produced by cosmic rays and have stopped in the Earth or some other
139: astronomical body, and the stability of these astronomical bodies means that they
140: cannot be dangerous.
141: 
142: In the case of the equally hypothetical strangelets, we review in Section 5 the data
143: accumulated at RHIC on the abundances and velocities of strongly-interacting
144: particles, including those containing one or more strange quarks, produced at RHIC
145: and in previous heavy-ion collision experiments [3]. All these data are very
146: consistent with a simple thermodynamic production mechanism that depends only
147: on the effective temperature and the net density of baryons (nucleons). The effective
148: temperature agrees well with first-principles theoretical calculations, and the net
149: density of baryons decreases as the energy increases, again in agreement with
150: theoretical calculations. Calculations for heavy-ion collisions at the LHC give a
151: similar effective temperature and a lower net density of baryons than at RHIC. This
152: means that the LHC could only produce strangelets at a lower rate, if they exist at
153: all.
154: 
155: We conclude by reiterating the conclusion of the LHC Safety Group in 2003 [1]:
156: there is no basis for any conceivable threat from the LHC. Indeed, theoretical and
157: experimental developments since 2003 have reinforced this conclusion.

164: 2 – The LHC compared with Cosmic-Ray Collisions
165: 
166: The LHC is designed to collide two counter-rotating beams of protons or heavy
167: ions. Proton-proton collisions are foreseen at an energy of 7 TeV per beam. An
168: equivalent energy in the centre of mass would be obtained in the collision of a
169: cosmic-ray proton with a fixed target such as the Earth or some other astronomical
170: body if its energy reaches or exceeds 108 GeV, i.e., 1017 eV [4]. When the LHC attains
171: its design collision rate, it will produce about a billion proton-proton collisions per
172: second in each of the major detectors ATLAS and CMS. The effective amount of
173: time each year that the LHC will produce collisions at this average luminosity is
174: about ten million seconds. Hence, each of the two major detectors is expecting to
175: obtain about 1017 proton-proton collisions over the planned duration of the
176: experiments.
177: 
178: As seen in Fig. 1, the highest-energy cosmic rays observed attain energies of around
179: 1020 eV, and the total flux of cosmic rays with energies of 1017 eV or more that hit
180: each square centimeter of the Earth’s surface is measured to be about 5x10–14 per
181: second [5]. The area of the Earth’s surface is about 5x1018 square centimeters, and
182: the age of the Earth is about 4.5 billion years. Therefore, over 3x1022 cosmic rays
183: with energies of 1017 eV or more, equal to or greater than the LHC energy, have
184: struck the Earth’s surface since its formation. This means [6] that Nature has
185: already conducted the equivalent of about a hundred thousand LHC experimental
186: programmes on Earth already – and the planet still exists.
187: 
188: 
189: 
190: 
191: Fig. 1: The spectrum of ultra-high-energy cosmic rays, as measured by several experiments
192: [5]. Every cosmic ray with an energy shown in this plot, namely above 1017 eV, liberates in
193: its collision with the atmosphere more energy in its centre-of-mass frame than does a proton-
194: proton collision at the LHC.
195: 
196: Other astronomical bodies are even larger. For example, the radius of Jupiter is
197: about ten times that of the Earth, and the radius of the Sun is a factor of ten larger
198: still. The surface area of the Sun is therefore 10,000 times that of the Earth, and
199: Nature has therefore already conducted the LHC experimental programme about
200: one billion times [6] via the collisions of cosmic rays with the Sun – and the Sun still
201: exists.
202:

204: 
205: 
206:                                              4
207: 
208: Moreover, our Milky Way galaxy contains about 1011 stars with sizes similar to our
209: Sun, and there are about 1011 similar galaxies in the visible Universe. Cosmic rays
210: have been hitting all these stars at rates similar to collisions with our own Sun. This
211: means that Nature has already completed about 1031 LHC experimental
212: programmes since the beginning of the Universe. Moreover, each second, the
213: Universe is continuing to repeat about 3x1013 complete LHC experiments. There is
214: no indication that any of these previous “LHC experiments” has ever had any
215: large-scale consequences. The stars in our galaxy and others still exist, and
216: conventional astrophysics can explain all the astrophysical black holes detected.
217: 
218: Thus, the continued existence of the Earth and other astronomical bodies can be
219: used to constrain or exclude speculations about possible new particles that might be
220: produced by the LHC.

222: 
223: 3 – Vacuum Bubbles and Magnetic Monopoles
224: 
225: These large rates for the collisions of cosmic rays at energies higher than the LHC
226: imply directly that there can be no danger to the Earth from the production of
227: bubbles of new vacuum or magnetic monopoles at the LHC [1].
228: 
229: It has often been suggested that the Universe might not be absolutely stable,
230: because the state that we call the ‘vacuum’ might not be the lowest-energy state. In
231: this case, our ‘vacuum’ would eventually decay into such a lower-energy state.
232: Since this has not happened, the lifetime before any such decay must be longer than
233: the age of the Universe. The possible concern about high-energy particle collisions
234: is that they might stimulate the production of small ‘bubbles’ of such a lower-
235: energy state, which would then expand and destroy not just the Earth, but
236: potentially the entire Universe.
237: 
238: However, if LHC collisions could produce vacuum bubbles, so also could cosmic-
239: ray collisions. This possibility was first studied in [7], and the conclusions drawn
240: there were reiterated in [8]. These bubbles of new vacuum would have expanded to
241: consume large parts of the visible Universe several billion years ago already. The
242: continued existence of the Universe means that such vacuum bubbles are not
243: produced in cosmic-ray collisions, and hence the LHC will also not produce any
244: vacuum bubbles.
245: 
246: There have also been suggestions over the years that there might exist magnetic
247: monopoles, particles with non-zero free magnetic charge. As was originally pointed
248: out by Dirac, any free magnetic charge would be quantized, and necessarily much
249: larger in magnitude than the electric charge of the electron or proton. For this
250: reason, searches for magnetic monopoles have looked for heavily-ionizing particles
251: as well as for quanta of magnetic charge, and this search will be continued at the
252: LHC.
253: 
254: In some grand unified theories, though not in the Standard Model of particle
255: physics, magnetic monopoles might also catalyze nucleon decay, by transforming
256: protons and neutrons into electrons or positrons and unstable mesons. In this case,
257: successive collisions with large numbers of nuclei would release considerable
258: energy. The magnetic monopoles that might have such properties are expected to
259: weigh 1015 GeV or more, far too heavy to be produced at the LHC. Nevertheless,
260: here we consider the possibility of producing light proton-eating magnetic
261: monopoles at the LHC.
262: 
263: 
264: 
265: 
266:                                           5
267: 
268: A quantitative discussion of the impact of such magnetic monopoles on Earth was
269: presented in [1], where it was concluded that only a microgram of matter would be
270: destroyed before the monopole exited the Earth. Independently of this conclusion,
271: if monopoles could be produced by the LHC, high-energy cosmic rays would
272: already have created many of them when striking the Earth and other astronomical
273: bodies. Since they would have large magnetic charges, any monopoles produced by
274: cosmic rays would have been stopped by the material of the Earth [2]. The
275: continued existence of the Earth and other astronomical bodies after billions of
276: years of high-energy cosmic-ray bombardment means that any monopoles
277: produced could not catalyze proton decay at any appreciable rate. If the collisions
278: made in the LHC could produce dangerous monopoles, high-energy cosmic rays
279: would already have done so.
280: 
281: The continued existences of the Earth and other astronomical bodies such as the
282: Sun mean that any magnetic monopoles produced by high-energy cosmic rays must
283: be harmless. Likewise, if any monopoles are produced at the LHC, they will be
284: harmless.
285: 
286: 4 – Microscopic Black Holes
287: 
288: As has already been discussed, the LHC will make collisions with a much lower
289: centre-of-mass energy than some of the cosmic rays that have been bombarding the
290: Earth and other astronomical bodies for billions of years. We estimate that, over the
291: history of the Universe, Nature has carried out the equivalent of 1031 LHC projects
292: (defined by the integrated luminosity for cosmic-ray collisions at a centre-of-mass
293: energy of 14 TeV or more), and continues to do so at the rate of over 1013 per
294: second, via the collisions of energetic cosmic rays with different astronomical
295: bodies.
296: 
297: There is, however, one significant difference between cosmic-ray collisions with a
298: body at rest and collisions at the LHC, namely that any massive new particles
299: produced by the LHC collisions will tend to have low velocities, whereas cosmic-
300: ray collisions would produce them with high velocities. This point has been
301: considered in detail [2] since the 2003 report by the LHC Safety Study Group [1]. As
302: we now discuss, the original conclusion that LHC collisions present no dangers is
303: validated and strengthened by this more recent work.
304: 
305: We recall that the black holes observed in the Universe have very large masses,
306: considerably greater than that of our Sun. On the other hand, each collision of a pair
307: of protons in the LHC will release an amount of energy comparable to that of two
308: colliding mosquitos, so any black hole produced would be much smaller than those
309: known to astrophysicists. In fact, according to the conventional gravitational theory
310: of General Relativity proposed by Einstein, many of whose predictions have
311: subsequently been verified, there is no chance that any black holes could be
312: produced at the LHC, since the conventional gravitational forces between
313: fundamental particles are too weak.
314: 
315: However, there are some theoretical speculations that, when viewed at very small
316: distances, space may reveal extra dimensions. In some such theories, it is possible
317: that the gravitational force between pairs of particles might become strong at the
318: energy of the LHC.
319: 
320: As was pointed out 30 years ago by Stephen Hawking [9], it is expected that all
321: black holes are ultimately unstable. This is because of very basic features of
322: quantum theory in curved spaces, such as those surrounding any black hole. The
323: 
324: 
325: 
326:                                           6
327: 
328: basic reason is very simple: it is a consequence of quantum mechanics that particle-
329: antiparticle pairs must be created near the event horizon surrounding any black
330: hole. Some particles (or antiparticles) disappear into the black hole itself, and the
331: corresponding antiparticles (or particles) must escape as radiation. There is broad
332: consensus among physicists on the reality of Hawking radiation, but so far no
333: experiment has had the sensitivity required to find direct evidence for it.
334: 
335: Independently of the reasoning based on Hawking radiation, if microscopic black
336: holes were to be singly produced by colliding the quarks and gluons inside protons,
337: they would also be able to decay into the same types of particles that produced
338: them [10]. The reason being that in this case they could not carry any conserved
339: quantum number that is not already carried by the original quarks and gluons, and
340: their decay back to the initial state partons would be allowed. For this reason, a
341: microscopic black hole cannot be completely black. In standard quantum physics,
342: the decay rate would be directly related to the production rate, and the expected
343: lifetime would be very short. The case of pair production of black holes carrying
344: new and opposite conserved quantum numbers leads to similar conclusions: only
345: their ground state is guaranteed to be stable, and any further accretion of normal
346: matter in the form of quarks, gluons or leptons would immediately be radiated
347: away. Both this and the existence of Hawking radiation are valid in the extra-
348: dimensional scenarios used to suggest the possible production of microscopic black
349: holes.
350: 
351: One might nevertheless wonder what would happen if a stable microscopic black
352: hole could be produced at the LHC [2]. However, we reiterate that this would
353: require a violation of some of the basic principles of quantum mechanics – which is
354: a cornerstone of the laws of Nature – in order for the black hole decay rate to be
355: suppressed relative to its production rate, and/or of general relativity – in order to
356: suppress Hawking radiation.
357: 
358: Most black holes produced at the LHC or in cosmic-ray collisions would have an
359: electric charge, since they would originate from the collisions of charged quarks. A
360: charged object interacts with matter in an experimentally well-understood way. A
361: direct consequence of this is that charged and stable black holes produced by the
362: interactions of cosmic rays with the Earth or the Sun would be slowed down and
363: ultimately stopped by their electromagnetic interactions inside these bodies, in spite
364: of their initial high velocities. The complete lack of any macroscopic effect caused
365: by stable black holes, which would have accumulated in the billions during the
366: lifetime of the Earth and the Sun if the LHC could produce them, means that either
367: they are not produced, or they are all neutral and hence none are stopped in the
368: Earth or the Sun, or have no large-scale effects even if they are stopped.
369: 
370: If a black hole were to be produced by a cosmic ray, as it traveled through the Earth
371: it would absorb preferentially similar numbers of protons and neutrons, because
372: their masses are larger than that of the electron. It would, therefore, develop and
373: maintain a positive charge, even if it were produced with no electric charge. The
374: standard neutralization process due to the quantum creation of particle-antiparticle
375: pairs near the horizon – the Schwinger mechanism – relies on principles very
376: similar to those at the basis of Hawking radiation, and would likely not operate if
377: the latter was suppressed. Thus, combining the hypotheses that black holes are
378: simultaneously neutral and stable and accrete matter requires some further
379: deviation from basic physical laws. There is no concrete example of a consistent
380: scenario for microphysics that would exhibit such behaviour. Furthermore, it is
381: possible [2] to exclude any macroscopic consequences of black holes even if such
382: unknown mechanisms were realized, as we now discuss.
383: 
384: 
385: 
386:                                           7
387: 
388: The rate at which any stopped black hole would absorb the surrounding material
389: and grow in mass is model-dependent. This is discussed in full detail in [2], where
390: several accretion scenarios, based on well-founded macroscpic physics, have been
391: used to set conservative, worst-case-scenario limits to the black hole growth rates in
392: the Earth and in denser bodies like white dwarfs and neutron stars. In the extra-
393: dimensional scenarios that motivate the existence of microscopic black holes (but
394: not their stability), the rate at which absorption would take place would be so slow
395: if there are seven or more dimensions that Earth would survive for billions of years
396: before any harm befell it. The reason is that in such scenarios the size of the extra
397: dimensions is very small, so small that the evolution driven by the strong extra-
398: dimensional gravity forces terminates while the growing black hole is still of
399: microscopic size. If there are only five or six dimensions of space-time relevant at
400: the LHC scale, on the other hand, the gravitational interactions of black holes are
401: strong enough that their impact, should they exist, would be detectable in the
402: Universe.
403: 
404: In fact, ultra-high-energy cosmic rays hitting dense stars such as white dwarfs and
405: neutron stars would have produced black holes copiously during their lifetimes.
406: Such black holes, even if neutral, would have been stopped by the material inside
407: such dense stars. The rapid accretion due to the large density of these bodies, and to
408: the strong gravitational interactions of these black holes, would have led to the
409: destruction of white dwarfs and neutron stars on time scales that are much shorter
410: than their observed lifetimes [2]. The final stages of their destruction would have
411: released explosively large amounts of energy, that would have been highly visible.
412: The observation of white dwarfs and neutron stars that would have been destroyed
413: in this way tells us that cosmic rays do not produce such black holes, and hence
414: neither will the LHC.
415: 
416: To conclude: in addition to the very general reasoning excluding the possibility that
417: stable black holes exist, and in particular that they could only be neutral, we
418: therefore have very robust empirical evidence either disproving their existence, or
419: excluding any consequence of it.
420: 
421: 5 - Strangelets
422: 
423: The research programme of the CERN Large Hadron Collider also includes the
424: collisions of ultra-relativistic lead and other nuclei (ions). The main scientific goal of
425: this heavy-ion programme is to produce matter at the highest temperatures and
426: densities attainable in the laboratory, and to study its properties. This programme is
427: expected to produce, in very small quantities, primordial plasma of the type that
428: filled the Universe when it was about a microsecond old.
429: 
430: The normal matter of which we are made, and which constitutes all the known
431: visible matter in the Universe, is composed of the two lightest types of quarks, the
432: up and down quarks. Heavier, unstable quarks have been discovered in cosmic-ray
433: collisions and at accelerators, and the lightest of these is the strange quark. Particles
434: containing strange quarks have been produced regularly in the laboratory for many
435: decades, and are known to decay on time scales of the order of a nanosecond, or
436: faster. Such lifetimes are characteristic of the weak interaction responsible for
437: radioactivity, which governs their decay. Some unstable particles containing two or
438: three strange quarks have also been observed. Particles including one strange quark
439: have been shown to bind to nuclei, the so-called hypernuclei, which are however
440: unstable and promptly decay, again with nanosecond time scales. Apart from
441: 
442: 
443: 
444: 
445:                                             8
446: 
447: rapidly decaying nuclei with two particles each containing one strange quark [11],
448: no nuclei containing multiple strange quarks are known.
449: 
450: Strange quark matter is a hypothetical state of matter, which would consist of large,
451: roughly equal numbers of up, down and strange quarks. Hypothetical small lumps
452: of strange quark matter, having atomic masses comparable to ordinary nuclei, are
453: often referred to as strangelets. As discussed in more detail in the Appendix, most
454: theoretical studies of strangelets conclude that, if they exist, they must be unstable,
455: decaying with a typical strange-particle lifetime of around a nanosecond. In this
456: case, any production of strangelets would pose no risk. However, it has been
457: speculated that strange quark matter might weigh less than conventional nuclear
458: matter with the same number of up and down quarks, but not for atomic numbers
459: smaller than 10. In this very hypothetical case, such a strangelet would be stable. It
460: has been further speculated that, if produced, strangelets could coalesce with
461: normal matter and catalyze its conversion into strange matter, thereby creating an
462: ever-growing strangelet. This hypothetical scenario underlies concerns about
463: strangelet production at accelerators, which were discussed previously in [8] and
464: [1].
465: 
466: It is generally expected that any stable strangelet would have a positive charge, in
467: which case it would be repelled by ordinary nuclear matter, and hence unable to
468: convert it into strange matter [8], see [12], however. In some model studies, one
469: finds that negatively-charged strangelets can also exist, but are unstable since the
470: positively-charged states have lower energy [13]. However, there is no rigorous
471: proof that the charge of a stable strangelet must be positive, nor that a negatively-
472: charged strangelet cannot be metastable, i.e., very long-lived. So, one should also
473: consider the possibility of a negatively-charged stable or very long-lived strangelet.
474: 
475: Prior to the start of the Relativistic Heavy-Ion Collider (RHIC), a study was carried
476: out [8] to assess hypothetical scenarios for the production of strangelets in heavy-
477: ion collisions. Additional arguments were given in [14], and a reassessment of such
478: a possibility was given in the 2003 Report of the LHC CERN Safety Study Group
479: [1]. We revisit here this topic in light of recent advances in our understanding of the
480: theory and experiment of heavy-ion collisions. These enable us to update and
481: strengthen the previous conclusions about hypothetical scenarios based on
482: strangelet production. More details of our considerations on strangelet production
483: at the LHC are given in the appendix.
484: 
485: The 2003 report summarized the status of direct experimental searches and of
486: theoretical speculations about hypothetical strangelet production mechanisms [1].
487: More recently, additional direct upper limits on strangelet production have been
488: provided by experimental searches at RHIC [15] and among cosmic rays [16], which
489: have not yielded any evidence for the existence of strangelets. In the near future,
490: additional experimental information may be expected from strangelet searches in
491: samples of lunar soil and from particle detectors in outer space [17].
492: 
493: On the theoretical side, the 2003 report considered three mechanisms for strangelet
494: production [1]: i) a thermal mechanism [3], in which particles are produced as if
495: from a heat bath in thermal equilibrium, ii) a coalescence mechanism, in which
496: particles produced in a heavy-ion collision might combine at late times to form a
497: strangelet, and iii) a distillation mechanism [18], which was proposed as a specific
498: model for strangelet production. According to this last mechanism, a hot quark-
499: gluon plasma with large net baryon number is produced in heavy-ion collisions,
500: and is enriched in strangeness as it cools down by emitting predominantly particles
501: containing strange antiquarks.
502: 
503: 
504: 
505:                                           9
506: 
507: As discussed in the Appendix, no evidence has been found in the detailed study of
508: heavy-ion collisions at RHIC for an anomalous coalescence mechanism. In
509: particular, the production rate of light nuclei measured in central Au+Au collisions
510: at RHIC [14], is consistent with the coalescence rates, used in the 2003 Report of the
511: LHC CERN Safety Study Group [1] to rule out strangelet production. There is also
512: considerable experimental evidence against the distillation mechanism. For this
513: mechanism to be operational, the produced matter should have a long lifetime and
514: a large net nucleon density. However, experiments at RHIC confirm the general
515: expectations that the net nucleon density is small and decreases at higher collision
516: energies. Moreover, the plasma produced in the collision is very short-lived,
517: expanding rapidly at about half the velocity of light, and falling apart within 10–23
518: seconds [19]. Furthermore, no characteristic difference has been observed in the
519: production of particles containing strange quarks and antiquarks. Hence, a
520: distillation mechanism capable of giving rise to strangelet production is not
521: operational in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC, and this suggestion for strange-particle
522: production has been abandoned for the LHC. On the other hand, as reviewed
523: below, RHIC data strongly support models that describe particle production as
524: emission from a high-temperature heat bath [3].
525: 
526: If they exist, strangelets would be bound states that would be formed initially with
527: an atomic number comparable to that of normal nuclei. Like normal nuclei,
528: strangelets would also contain a significant baryon number. We know from the
529: basic principles of quantum mechanics that, for a strangelet to be formed, its
530: constituents must be assembled in a configuration that contains less than its
531: characteristic binding energy. If this were not the case, the forces between the
532: constituents would not be strong enough to hold them together, and the strangelet
533: would not form. As a consequence, strangelet formation is less likely if the
534: constituents have initially more kinetic energy, and specifically if they emerge from
535: a hotter system. Correspondingly, strangelet production is less likely in a hotter
536: system.
537: 
538: The energy needed to break up a strangelet is similar to that needed to break up a
539: normal nucleus, which is of the order of one to a few million electron volts. Similar
540: energies would be reached in a heat bath with a temperature of ten to several tens
541: of billions of degrees Celsius. However, heavy-ion collisions are known to produce
542: heat baths that are far hotter, reaching temperatures exceeding 1 trillion degrees
543: Celsius [3]. Basic thermodynamics would require most strangelets to melt in such a
544: heat bath, i.e., dissociate into the known strange particles that decay within a
545: nanosecond. For this reason, the likelihood of strangelet production in relativistic
546: heavy-ion collisions can be compared to the likelihood of producing an icecube in a
547: furnace.
548: 
549: The analogy of heavy-ion collisions with a particle furnace has been supported by
550: many detailed measurements in accelerator collisions of the production of different
551: types of particles, including those containing one, two or three strange quarks. Fig.
552: 2 shows one such piece of evidence: the relative rates at which particles are
553: produced in heavy-ion collisions at RHIC is in line with a theoretical calculation
554: assuming a furnace with a temperature around 1.6 trillion degrees [3]. All particle
555: ratios are well-described, including rare particles like the Omega baryon, which
556: contains three strange quarks and which – if compared to the most abundant
557: particles such as pions - is produced only at the per-mille level (see Fig. 2).
558: Supplementary information is provided in the Appendix.

574: The total number of heavy-ion collisions created at the LHC will be comparable to
575: the total number of heavy ion collisions created at RHIC. The LHC will be at least as
576: hot a furnace as RHIC, in the sense that the systems produced in heavy-ion

578: produced at RHIC. This is one factor that makes strangelet production no more
579: likely at the LHC than at RHIC. Another major factor pointing in the same direction
580: is that the net density of nucleons, measured by the baryon number, will be lower
581: at the LHC than at RHIC. This is because the system produced in heavy-ion
582: collisions at the LHC is spread over a larger rapidity range, and the same total net
583: baryon number will be spread over a larger volume. As discussed in more detail in
584: the Appendix, this effect has already been seen at RHIC, where the net density of
585: nucleons is lower than in lower-energy experiments, and this trend will continue at
586: the LHC [3]. Since strangelets require baryon number to be formed, this effect
587: makes strangelet production less likely at the LHC than at RHIC.
588: 
589: We conclude on general physical grounds that heavy-ion collisions at the LHC are
590: less likely to produce strangelets than the lower-energy heavy-ion collisions already
591: carried out in recent years at RHIC, just as strangelet production at RHIC was less
592: likely than in previous lower-energy experiments carried out in the 1980s and 1990s
593: [8].
594: 
595: Knowing that strangelet production at the LHC is less likely than at previous lower-
596: energy machines, we now review the arguments that strangelet production in
597: previous lower-energy experiments did not pose any conceivable risk.
598: 
599: 
600: 
601:                                              11
602: 
603: It has been shown that the continuing survival of the Moon under cosmic-ray
604: bombardment ensures that heavy-ion collisions do not pose any conceivable threat
605: via strangelet production [8]. This is because cosmic rays have a significant
606: component of heavy ions, as does the surface of the Moon. Since the Moon, unlike
607: planets such as the Earth, is not protected by an atmosphere, cosmic rays hitting the
608: Moon have produced heavy-ion collisions over billions of years at energies that are
609: comparable to or exceed those reached in man-made experiments.
610: 
611: The conclusion reached in [8] required two well-motivated assumptions. Since
612: high-energy cosmic rays include many iron nuclei, which are also prevalent in the
613: Moon’s surface, it was assumed that the conditions reached in iron-iron collisions
614: are comparable to those reached in the collisions of gold ions or lead ions that had
615: been studied previously in the laboratory. Secondly, since RHIC and LHC
616: experiments take place in the centre-of-mass reference frame, whereas in cosmic-ray
617: collisions the centre-of-mass frame is moving at high speed, it was necessary to
618: make some assumption about the velocity distribution of any strangelets produced.
619: We recall that high-velocity strangelets might well be broken up by lunar matter
620: before becoming slow enough to coalesce with it.
621: 
622: Since the appearance of [8], the RHIC heavy-ion programme has also studied the
623: collisions of the copper ions, which are closely comparable to iron-iron collisions.
624: The abundances of particles produced in these collisions are described by the same
625: thermal model of a particle furnace that accounts successfully for particle
626: production in gold-gold collisions. Moreover, the velocity distributions of all
627: particle species observed at RHIC are similar to or broader than the distribution
628: assumed in [8]. These observations support the assumptions made in [8], and
629: therefore strengthen their conclusions.
630: 
631: An independent safety argument, which does not require any assumption about the
632: velocity distribution of any hypothetical strangelets, has been given in [20]. The rate
633: of cosmic-ray heavy-ion collisions in interstellar space is known. If these collisions
634: produced any strangelets, these would have accreted in stars and any large-scale
635: coalescence would have resulted in stellar explosions that have not been seen. This
636: complementary argument does, however, assume that any strangelets produced do
637: not decay on a time scale much shorter than that of star formation.
638: 
639: We close this section by summarizing that the successful description of heavy-ion
640: collisions as a particle furnace with a net density of baryons that decreases at higher
641: energies implies that strangelet production at the LHC is less likely than at lower-
642: energy machines (see appendix). The arguments given previously for the safety of
643: lower-energy collisions are strengthened by recent observations at RHIC.
644: Furthermore, we note that the analogy of the LHC with a hot particle furnace will
645: be monitored from the earliest days of heavy-ion collisions at the LHC. A thousand
646: heavy-ion collisions would already suffice for a first test of the thermal model
647: which describes heavy-ion collisions as a particle furnace. This will be among the
648: first data analyses done in the LHC heavy-ion programme, and will immediately
649: provide an experimental confirmation of the basic assumptions on which the safety
650: argument is based.

652: 6 – Conclusions
653: 
654: Having reviewed the theoretical and experimental developments since the previous
655: safety report was published, we confirm its findings. There is no basis for any
656: concerns about the consequences of new particles or forms of matter that could
657: possibly be produced by the LHC.
658: 
659: 
660: 
661:                                           12
662: 
663: In the case of phenomena, such as vacuum bubble formation via phase transitions
664: or the production of magnetic monopoles, which had already been excluded by the
665: previous report [1], no subsequent development has put into question those firm
666: conclusions. Stable and neutral black holes, in addition to being excluded by all
667: known theoretical frameworks, are either excluded by the stability of astronomical
668: bodies, or would accrete at a rate that is too low to cause any macroscopic effects on
669: timescales much longer than the natural lifetime of the solar system. The previous
670: arguments about the impossibility to produce strangelets at the LHC are confirmed
671: and reinforced by the analysis of the RHIC data.
672: 
673: We have considered all the proposed speculative scenarios for new particles and
674: states of matter that currently raise safety issues. Since our methodology is based on
675: empirical reasoning based on experimental observations, it would be applicable to
676: other exotic phenomena that might raise concerns in the future.

684: In Section 5 we state that there is no basis for any conceivable threat from strangelet
685: production at the LHC. Our arguments follow closely the line of reasoning of
686: previous reports [8], but they put particular emphasis on two observations. First, on
687: general grounds, the probability for strangelet production decreases with increasing
688: center-of-mass energy. As a consequence, strangelet production at LHC is less likely
689: than at RHIC, just as it was less likely at RHIC than in the heavy-ion programs at
690: lower center-of-mass energies pursued in the 1980s and 1990s. Secondly, RHIC
691: data strongly disfavour models of particle production which were advocated as
692: production mechanisms for strangelets. On the contrary, RHIC data give strong
693: support to a thermal model of particle production, which puts tight upper bounds
694: on strangelet production. In this Appendix, we provide background information to
695: support these statements and the main conclusions drawn from them. In particular,
696: we recall the main arguments of the safety reports [8], and we discuss how these
697: arguments can be strengthened in the light of recent data from RHIC.

699: Strangelet properties
700: 
701: Strange quark matter is a hypothetical state of matter consisting of roughly equal
702: numbers of up, down and strange quarks. It has been speculated that strange quark
703: matter might constitute the true ground state of baryonic matter, being more stable
704: than ordinary nuclei [21,22]. Hypothetical small lumps of strange quark matter,
705: having atomic masses comparable to ordinary nuclei, are often referred to as
706: strangelets. Such strangelets might be either stable or metastable. At present, a first
707: principle theory of strange quark matter is not within theoretical reach. It would
708: require major theoretical breakthroughs in the application of QCD to finite density
709: and to mesoscopic systems. As a consequence, theoretical studies on whether
710: strangelets can exist for some parameter range depend on model-dependent
711: assumptions. As reviewed in detail in ref. [8], theoretical speculations about the
712: existence of strangelets may be summarized as follows:
713: 
714:    1. It is unclear whether bulk strange quark matter exists at all.
715: 
716:    2. It is unclear whether bulk strange quark matter can be stable. If it does exist,
717:       strange quark matter may be absolutely stable in bulk at zero external
718:       pressure, though the expected values for the relevant parameters tend to
719:       disfavour stability [1].
720: 
721:    3. Finite size effects make it very unlikely that small strangelets (A < 10) can be stable
722:       or long-lived. Even if bulk strange quark matter is stable, finite-size effects
723:       (surface tension and curvature) significantly destablize strangelets with low
724:       baryon number. For typical parameters, it has been estimated that finite-size
725:       effects add, e.g., 50 MeV per baryon for A = 20 and 85 MeV per baryon for
726:       A=10 [1].
727: 
728:    4. Stable strangelets, if they exist, could be present only in states of low entropy (i.e.,
729:       temperature). Hot strangelets are much less stable than cold ones. The
730:       characteristic scale to decide what is hot or cold is set by the binding energy
731:       per baryon of the strangelet. On general thermodynamic grounds, the time-
732:       scale for evaporation of hot strangelets is expected to be very small, though
733:       difficult to calculate. Assuming typical nuclear binding energies of O(1)
734:       MeV, one expects that stable strangelets need to be much colder than the
735:       matter produced in heavy-ion collisions [1,8].
736: 
737: 
738: 
739:                                              14
740: 
741:    5. If stable strangelets exist, they are most likely positively charged. If strange matter
742:       contained equal numbers of u, d and s quarks, it would be electrically
743:       neutral. Since, s quarks are heavier, Fermi gas kinematics alone indicates that
744:       strange quarks are suppressed, giving strange matter a positive charge per
745:       unit baryon number. However, the effects of gluon exchange reactions are
746:       difficult to quantify. Perturbatively, gluon exchange is repulsive and
747:       increases the mass. But gluon interactions weaken as quark masses are
748:       increased, so the gluonic repulsion is smaller between s-s, s-u or s-d pairs
749:       than between u and d quarks. Hence, increasing the strength of gluon
750:       interactions makes the charge of quark matter negative, but it also unbinds it.
751:       Unreasonably low values of the bag constant are necessary to compensate for
752:       a large repulsive gluonic interaction energy, which is why negatively-
753:       charged strangelets are regarded as extremely unlikely [8].
754: 
755: Hypothetical disaster scenarios based on strangelet production in heavy-ion
756: collisions require that strangelets be stable or very long-lived, and hence that they
757: are sufficiently cold. It has been argued in detail [8] that each of these conditions is
758: unlikely. In the following, we discuss in particular how RHIC data allow us to
759: strengthen the argument that sufficiently cold strangelets cannot be produced in the
760: hot particle furnace created in a heavy ion collision. Moreover, most hypothetical
761: disaster scenarios require that the produced strangelet be charged negatively, so
762: that its fusion with positively charged nuclei could lead to a hypothetically
763: disastrous chain of events. In contrast, in normal matter, positively-charged
764: strangelets would capture electrons, which would shield any fusion with other
765: nuclei. To trigger a run-away reaction in the latter case, one must invoke an
766: ionizing mechanism, e.g., by transporting the strangelets to the interior of the Sun
767: [12], and this adds another layer of unlikely assumptions.
768:

885: that the suppression factor for production of an A = 20 nuclei in a central heavy ion
886: collision is between 10-53 and 10-46. This compares very well with the suppression
887: factor of order 10-49 obtained in the above discussion of thermal models.
888: 
889: Since coalescence models do not differ qualitatively from thermal models, the same
890: safety arguments apply. For this reason, we emphasize in the text that in the
891: detailed study of heavy-ion collisions at RHIC and lower energies, no evidence for
892: an anomalous coalescence mechanism has been found. The basis of the 2003 report
893: has been fully vindicated by further RHIC running.
894: 
895: 
896: 3. Distillation mechanism
897: 
898: Strangeness distillation has been proposed specifically as a mechanism for
899: strangelet production. This mechanism assumes that a baryon-rich quark-gluon
900: plasma is produced in a heavy-ion collision, which cools by evaporation from its
901: surface. Due to the large baryon chemical potential in this plasma, an s antiquark
902: would be more likely to pair with a u or d quark, than an s quark with a u or d
903: antiquark. As a consequence, the cooling of the plasma would lead to an excess of s
904: quarks in a baryon-rich lump, which may finally become a strangelet.
905:                                                                 !
906: We note that this production process would be more likely for              ! baryon
907:                                                                      ! large
908: chemical potential, and thus would be less likely for heavy-ion collisions at the
909: LHC than at lower center-of-mass energies. Moreover, there is by now significant
910: empirical evidence against a dynamical picture of heavy-ion collisions in which
911: strangeness distillation could be operational. In particular, empirical evidence from
912: 
913: 
914:                                           18
915: 
916: RHIC strongly supports explosive production scenarios, in which, for instance,
917: collective-flow gradients increase with center-of-mass energy [26]. The short
918: lifetime of the produced systems (of the order of 10 fm/c) is not expected to allow
919: for an evaporation process. Moreover, the explosive collective dynamics is expected
920: to favor bulk emission rather than surface emission [26]. So, there is no evidence for
921: a distillation mechanism capable of strangelet production at RHIC, and this
922: suggestion for strange particle production has been abandoned for the LHC.
923: 
924: Direct experimental searches for strangelets
925: 
926: Strangelets have been searched for in ordinary matter on Earth [27] and in heavy-
927: ion collisions over a wide range of center-of-mass energies. In particular, searches
928: for stra
929: lets have been reported by several experiments at the Brookhaven Alternating
930: Gradient Synchrotron [28], by the NA52 Collaboration at the SPS [29], and by the
931: STAR Collaboration at RHIC [15]. All of these searches yielded negative results and
932: reported complementary upper limits. In particular, STAR reported an upper limit
933: of less than 10-6 strangelets per central Au-Au collision for strangelets with lifetimes
934: larger than 0.1 ns and mass larger than 30 GeV. More details about the experimental
935: situation can be found in the previous reports [1,8].

937: Summary of the safety argument
938: 
939: 1. Quantitative considerations
940: 
941: The maximal luminosity of lead-lead (Pb+Pb) collisions at the LHC is L = 1027 cm-2 s-
942: 1
943:   . With a hadronic Pb+Pb cross section of 8 barn, this implies a rate of up to 8000
944: Pb+Pb collisions per second. With a foreseen running time of 1 month per year (106
945: seconds) times a duration of the program of, say, 10 years, we arrive at a
946: conservative upper bound on the total number of ion-ion collisions at the LHC of
947: O(1011). However, a large fraction of the hadronic Pb+Pb cross section is diffractive
948: or very peripheral. Only 10 percent of the entire rate can be considered as being
949: sufficiently central for creating a collision system characteristic of a heavy-ion
950: collision with a number of participants Npart > 20. As a consequence, a conservative
951: bound on the number of heavy ion collisions relevant for production of an A = 10
952: nucleus is O(1010).
953: 
954: Our conservative estimate for the thermal production of a normal A = 10 nucleus at
955: the LHC was 3 " 10-25 times the rate of nucleon production. Taking the latter rate to
956: lie in the hundreds, we arrive at a probability of 10-13 that a single normal nucleus
957: of size A = 10 is produced during the entire LHC program as a result of the
958: essentially thermal dynamics in a heavy ion collision. So, if LHC would run for the
959: entire lifetime of the Universe, the probability of producing such a single nucleus
960: via thermal production would be 1/10001.
961: 
962: We note that the above is an estimate for the thermal production of a normal A = 10
963: nucleus from a hadron gas of temperature T = 165 MeV. The production of normal
964: nuclear matter provides an extremely conservative upper bound on the production
965: of strange quark matter. For this reason, we find that the significant empirical
966: support for thermal particle production in heavy ion collisions, which was
967: substantiated further by RHIC data in recent years, strengthens the main conclusion
968: 
969: 1
970:   One may add that in semi-peripheral collisions, nuclei with A = 10 may appear amongst the break-
971: up products of the spectators of the nuclear projectile. However, such fragment production of
972: nuclear remnants is not a mechanism that could give rise to strangelets. For this reason, we focus
973: solely on thermal production rates of normal nuclei.

980: production at the LHC.
981: 
982: 2. Qualitative considerations
983: 
984: The above estimate of an upper limit to the probability of A = 10 nuclei can be
985: further strengthened by the following qualitative argument, which is based on
986: general principles of thermodynamics alone.
987: 
988:       Strangelets are cold, dense systems. Like nuclei, they are bound by O(1-10) of
989: MeV (if they are bound at all). Heavy-ion collisions produce hot systems. At LHC,
990: the temperatures reached are in excess of 100 MeV. The second law of
991: thermodynamics fights against the condensation of a system an order of magnitude
992: colder than the surrounding medium. The hypothetical production of a cold
993: strangelet from a hot hadron gas has been compared to producing an ice cube in a
994: furnace [8].
995: 
996:         This paper has aimed at communicating this central qualitative idea. In this
997: appendix, we have provided the quantitative background, to which the notion of
998: “particle furnace” corresponds. As seen from Fig. 4, measurements show that
999: heavy-ion collisions reach temperatures of T = 160 MeV in the last stage of the
1000: collision. Moreover, the baryon chemical potential, which characterizes the net
1001: quark density, decreases as the center-of-mass energy increases, further decreasing
1002: the likelihood of producing any system with large atomic number. The particle
1003: abundances measured at RHIC and in lower-energy experiments are consistent
1004: with expectations from the thermal model of statistical hadronization (see Fig. 3).
1005: This model is also known to apply to the production of light nuclei, as far as they
1006: have been identified experimentally, and it provides a very large suppression factor
1007: for the production of A = 10 nuclei. On these grounds, we conclude that the
1008: experimental evidence from RHIC for the thermal model of particle production
1009: significantly strengthens the conclusions of the 2003 report of the LHC Safety Study
1010: Group.
