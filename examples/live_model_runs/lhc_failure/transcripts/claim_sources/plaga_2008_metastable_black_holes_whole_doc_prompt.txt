48: 1.1 General outline of the problem
49: 
50: 
51: Theories with “extra spatial dimensions”[33], are one of the most popular
52: extensions of the standard model of particle physics and a central plank
53: of string theory[17]. If space had more than three dimensions the “Planck
54: energy scale” - usually thought to lie at extremely high energies - could
55: be reached already at energies projected for new particle accelerators[2,37].
56: Gravity becomes strong near the Planck energy. As a consequence subnu-
57: clear “micro” black holes (mBHs) could be copiously produced at future high-
58: energy particle colliders[14,33], such as the “Large Hadron Collider” (LHC)
59: at CERN. At a predicted rate of up to about one BH per second at the nom-
60: inal LHC luminosity[14], the LHC would be a “black-hole factory”[18,3]. The
61: phenomenology of mBHs at colliders has been studied in great detail, see e.g.
62: Cavaglia et al.[6].
63: The possibility that a collider-produced black hole (BH) - or another exotic ob-
64: ject - might catastrophically grow by gravitationally pulling in ambient matter
65: and thus eventually injure or kill humans deserves careful attention[43,30,9,38].
66: A recent scientific comparative study of global risks[34] has put a risk very
67: similar to the one considered here (from collider-produced “strangelets”) at
68: the top “response priority” of all current “untreated risks” (such as, for ex-
69: ample, super-volcano eruptions and asteroid impacts). Clearly this potential
70: risk exists only if speculative theories are correct. But these theories were con-
71: structed to explore real possibilities. The probability that they are correct is
72: not negligible.

411: 3.4 A local accident at CERN?
412: 
413: 
414: The luminosity of a mBH accreting at the Eddington limit with the parameters
415: assumed above corresponds to 12 Mt TNT equivalent/sec[15], or the energy
416: released in a major thermonuclear explosion per second. If such a mBH would
417: accrete near the surface of Earth the damage they create would be much larger
418: than deep in its interior. With the very small accretion timescale (≪ 1 second)
419: that was found with the parameters in subsection 3.2, a mBH created with
420: very small (thermal or subthermal) velocities in a collider would appear like a
421: major nuclear explosion in the immediate vicinity of the collider. The risk from
422: collider-produced black holes is not necessarily an Armageddon, but could be
423: a locally contained catastrophe.

427: 
428: 
429: If black holes are described by scenario 3 the Hawking radiation from collider-
430: produced black holes might be dangerous. The input parameters used in this
431: section were only one example. It can however be shown that there is a wide
432: range of values for L and MN that lead to dangerous mBHs accreting at the
433: Eddington limit with various luminosities.
434: In general the example developed above demonstrates that widely held in-
435: tuitions - namely that accretion by mBHs must be extremely slow[5], and
436: that events which are catastrophic for Earth must also be for compact stel-
437: lar objects (necessary for any safety argument based on such objects) - are
438: insufficient as safety guarantees.

456:

460: Thereby G & M introduce the assumption that mBHs in general have a mini-
461: mal mass Mmin that exceeds the new Planck scale by at least a factor of 3. This
462: constraint is motivated by the fact that the thermodynamical, semiclassical
463: treatment of mBHs in their scenario 2 is expected to be reliable within this
464: mass range. This is certainly a most reasonable argument for all purposes of
465: pure research, e.g. when predicting collider signatures etc.. However, it does
466: not mean that mBHs below Mmin cannot be produced. It rather means that
467: we are presently unable to reliably predict the behaviour of such mBHs 12 .
468: This fact raises a fundamental doubt about G & M’s exclusion of “danger-
469: ous mBHs” by way of observationally constraining the age of a certain class of
470: white dwarfs. This exclusion depends on their careful and detailed demonstra-
471: tion in their section 5 that “dangerous” mBHs are stopped in white dwarfs

473: tion is based on an assumed validity of the semiclassical approximation. mBHs
474: deep in the “quantum gravity” regime (violating eq.(7)) might have smaller
475: scattering cross section than expected in the semiclassically and escape white
476: dwarfs, just as they could escape ordinary stars 13 . This would void G & M’s
477: exclusion of the existence of potentially “dangerous” black holes.
478: Concluding, G & M did not demonstrate with reasonable certainty that white
479: dwarfs stop cosmic-ray produced mBHs in general. Their exclusion of danger-
480: ous mBHs thus remains not definite.

523: 5.2 Proposal for risk mitigating measures
524: 
525: 
526: It is not the aim of the present paper to recommend or discuss consequences
527: for the future operation of colliders comprehensively. Here I just put up for
528: further discussion three feasible measures for risk mitigation, at least in the
529: start up phase of LHC:
530: 1. Increase of collision energy by reasonably small factors (say, 2) in one step
531: and proceed to higher energies only after excluding any indication for poten-
532: tially dangerous processes. Currently it is planned to perform the first produc-
533: tion runs at LHC at an energy more than five times higher than previously
534: reached[35] 14 . This might result in the copious production of completely novel
535: states, which production was exponentially suppressed at the previous ener-
536: gies. “Proceeding in small steps” mitigates this risk.
537: 2. No operation in which no or only a very tiny fraction of events are analysed.
538: Currently it is planned to eventually record and analyse only a fraction of 10−7
539: of all events[40]. This is the equivalent of entering new territory and to be on
540: the lookout only for the interesting but not the potentially dangerous.
541: 3. Safety considerations influence the trigger and operational procedures. Meta
542: stable black holes might not yield very spectacular events, but it seems de-
543: sirable to ensure that their presence is immediately and reliably detected. An
544: immediate interruption of operation and detailed off-line study of the event
545: might be a possible risk-mitigating measure.
