# Rules

- Read the whole document before choosing claims.
- Return 3 to 3 canonical claims for this source, not one claim per paragraph or table row.
- A canonical claim should be something a decision analyst would want in the main evidence map.
- Split claims when the source reports materially distinct populations, endpoints, stated dose thresholds, or applicability boundaries.
- Prefer one canonical claim per distinct result cluster until the claim budget is reached.
- Result clusters include measured outcomes, harms, benefits, costs, operational effects, subgroup results, mechanism measures, implementation constraints, legal or policy consequences, and source-stated guidance.
- If the same endpoint cluster has materially different populations or dose thresholds, split those into separate claims.
- Keep related statistics together only when they answer the same population-endpoint-dose proposition.
- Do not merge blood pressure, blood lipids, mortality, major cardiovascular events, subgroup populations, and final guidance into one claim when the source distinguishes them.
- Turn table results into interpretable claims with row/column context; treat headings, references, and labels as context.
- If a table result matters, combine row/column context into one interpretable claim and cite a table-adjacent quote or narrative sentence.
- Preserve key quantities inside the relevant canonical claim rather than as separate claims.
- For quantities, prefer objects with value, quantity_role, measures, local_interpretation, source_quote, line_hint, and retention_hint.
- Use retention_hint must_retain only when the memo would be materially worse without that number; otherwise use use_if_space or audit_only.
- For each claim, fill claim_context with descriptive source-local fields likely to be stated in the source: population, exposure_or_option, outcome_or_endpoint, evidence_design, stated_dose_or_threshold, stated_scope, stated_limitations, and applicability_limits.
- Use empty strings or an empty applicability_limits list when the source does not state a descriptive field; do not guess.
- natural_bottom_line should be a concise reader-facing version of this claim, not a final answer to the whole decision question.
- must_preserve_terms should list exact populations, endpoints, quantities, comparators, and caveats that must survive later compression.
- Assign only extraction fields here; later stages assign support, counterweight, crux, scope, mechanism, and final map roles after an overall answer frame exists.
- For every canonical claim, set question_relevance and scope_flags to explain why it belongs in this decision map.
- Use decision_importance sparingly: critical only if the claim would materially change the answer or confidence.
- supporting_quotes must be exact substrings from the document; line_hint can be approximate, such as lines 50-52.
- Output JSON only.

4: SPC REPORT ON LSAG DOCUMENTS
5: 
6: The CERN Scientific Policy Committee (SPC) was asked by the President of the CERN Council to
7: examine the documents produced by the LHC Safety Assessment Group (LSAG) and to provide
8: Council with an independent opinion on the conclusions stated in those documents. A dedicated
9: SPC panel was set up, composed of Peter Braun-Munzinger, Matteo Cavalli-Sforza, Gerard ‘t
10: Hooft, Bryan Webber and Fabio Zwirner. The documents made available to the panel were:
11: "Review of the Safety of LHC Collisions", by the LHC Safety Assessment Group (LSAG report);
12: "Astronomical Implications of Hypothetical Stable TeV-Scale Black Holes", by S.B.Giddings and
13: M.L.Mangano (GM paper). After thorough review, discussion and ensuing endorsement by the
14: full SPC, the requested opinion is summarized in the present document.
15: 
16: Introduction
17: 
18: When new accelerators such as the LHC are built, exploring higher energies than their
19: predecessors, it is legitimate to ask whether there is any danger related to the production
20: of new particles or states of matter not previously created artificially. Such a question
21: was already addressed in the past: by an ad hoc Committee for the Relativistic Heavy Ion
22: Collider at the Brookhaven National Laboratory (RHIC) in 1999, and by the LHC Safety
23: Study Group at CERN (LSSG) in 2003. Both reports concluded that there was no danger.
24: The following objects of concern were considered, with emphasis on the last two,
25: discussed in both reports: magnetic monopoles, vacuum bubbles, strangelets, black holes.
26: 
27: Concerns have recently been expressed on the possibility that microscopic, stable, neutral
28: black holes may be produced at the LHC and then accrete matter sufficiently fast to pose
29: a risk to Earth. This highly hypothetical possibility had already been excluded on the
30: basis of very strong arguments, and most experts of the field consider this scenario as
31: impossible. However, the LSAG report now provides, on the basis of the GM paper, a
32: much stronger safety proof, relying only on solid experimental facts and firmly
33: established theory, which should remove any residual concern. More generally, the
34: LSAG report reviews the previous safety studies in the light of additional experimental
35: results and theoretical understanding, and is able to confirm, update and extend the LSSG
36: conclusions as described below.
37: 
38: No other exotic particles whose existence has been discussed in the scientific literature
39: would pose any potential danger at the LHC. The methodology used by LSAG would be
40: applicable to the evaluation of possible risks associated with other exotic particles or new
41: states of matter that might be suggested in the future.
42: 
43: The LHC compared with cosmic-ray collisions
44: 
45: Section 2 of the LSAG report shows quantitatively that collisions of cosmic rays of the
46: highest energies with the Earth, the Sun and other astronomical bodies occur naturally at
47: energies equivalent to or larger than those of LHC collisions, and in numbers that exceed
48: them by extremely large factors. The results and the considerations given in this section
49: are the basis for the safety proofs given later in the paper.
50: 
51:                                                                                            2
52: 
53: 
54: 
55: The starting point of the proof is the observed and well-known flux (per square cm per
56: second) of cosmic rays at center-of-mass energies that exceed those at the LHC by one to
57: a few factors of 10. Considering the surfaces of the target planets and stars and their
58: observed age, it is easily shown that such energetic LHC-like cosmic rays have already
59: hit the Earth (or the Sun) in numbers equivalent to one hundred thousand (or one billion)
60: times the total number of collisions expected over the duration of LHC experiments.
61: Analogously, in the whole Universe, the equivalent of 3·1013 complete LHC experiments
62: are performed every second. As shown in the LSAG report, and reviewed below, the
63: hypothesis that the LHC operation would threaten the existence of the Earth is
64: irreconcilable with the fact that the Earth, the Sun and other objects in the Cosmos have
65: persisted for billions of years in spite of their continuous exposure to energetic cosmic
66: rays.
67: 
68: The comparison between cosmic ray collisions with target planets or stars and LHC
69: collisions makes use of special relativity. Special relativity has been tested accurately in
70: the laboratory for particles with velocities similar to and higher than the relative velocity
71: of the two relevant reference frames; moreover, the experimental data on cosmic rays at
72: energies up to more than an order of magnitude above the equivalent LHC energy are in
73: agreement with special relativity.
74: 
75: Vacuum bubbles and magnetic monopoles
76: 
77: We fully endorse the conclusions of the LSAG report concerning the possible production
78: of bubbles of new vacuum or magnetic monopoles, discussed in Section 3. The
79: continuous bombardment of the Earth and other astronomical bodies by cosmic rays at
80: centre-of-mass energies at and above that of the LHC means that such processes, if they
81: exist, have occurred and are still occurring at a high rate throughout the Universe.
82: 
83: In the case of vacuum bubbles, the velocity of the object, once formed, is not relevant as
84: it would have expanded and caused visible large-scale effects, whatever its state of
85: motion and wherever it appeared, even in distant galaxies. Therefore no such objects are
86: ever created within the energy range of cosmic ray collisions.
87: 
88: It is worth remarking in this context that head-on collisions between cosmic rays, at
89: centre-of-mass energies enormously greater than those that will ever be accessible in
90: Earth-based experiments, occur sufficiently frequently on cosmic scales that we can be
91: completely confident that catastrophic disruption of the vacuum will never be a
92: possibility at the LHC or any future accelerator.
93: 
94: In the case of magnetic monopoles, such objects could have been produced in cosmic ray
95: collisions, but they are by definition highly ionizing and would therefore have been
96: stopped inside the Earth or any other target body. The continuing existence of
97: astronomical bodies shows that, if produced, magnetic monopoles must be harmless.

103: Microscopic black holes
104: 
105: Section 4 of the LSAG report addresses potential dangers associated with the production
106: of microscopic black holes at the LHC. For the cases that were already considered in
107: previous reports, it confirms their conclusions that there is absolutely no danger. In
108: addition, Section 4 reviews the highly hypothetical production of neutral stable black
109: holes, and the possibility that these black holes could be trapped inside the Earth. While
110: such a possibility is dismissed by most of the experts, it is conceivable, and it could have
111: potentially dangerous consequences if these highly hypothetical, neutral, stable
112: microscopic black holes could accrete matter on Earth on time scales shorter than the
113: natural lifetime of the solar system. For this to occur, a number of increasingly unlikely
114: conditions should be satisfied: 1) the fundamental scale of gravity should be within the
115: LHC energy range, possible only if extra spatial dimensions with very peculiar
116: properties do exist; 2) Hawking radiation, and more general arguments for the decay of
117: any black holes produced at the LHC, should fail; 3) the Schwinger mechanism, by which
118: a charged black hole becomes electrically neutral via particle-antiparticle creation near
119: the horizon, should still work, even though it is based on physical principles very close to
120: those underlying Hawking radiation.
121: 
122: Going beyond the RHIC and LSSG reports, the above-mentioned possibility is now
123: directly addressed by the LSAG report, on the basis of the GM paper, which is the most
124: detailed and specialized presentation of all the scientific evidence we have on this issue.
125: The GM paper admits from the start the possibility that compelling but not
126: experimentally tested mechanisms, such as Hawking radiation, could be suppressed or
127: absent. It chooses instead a conservative or "worst-case" scenario at every instance where
128: an experimental or theoretical uncertainty is encountered. It then uses observational data
129: on cosmic rays and on astronomical objects such as the Sun or compact stars to exclude
130: any danger at the LHC.
131: 
132: In particular, at the LHC energy, any danger for the Earth on time scales lower than or
133: comparable to the natural lifetime of the solar system can be ruled out on the basis of its
134: contradiction with the observation of white dwarf stars of known mass, age and other
135: properties. This conclusion, while entirely valid for the LHC, would need further work to
136: be extended to conceivable future colliders of much higher energies. A powerful
137: argument applicable also to higher energies is formulated making reference to observed
138: neutron stars, but this argument relies on properties of cosmic rays and neutrinos that,
139: while highly plausible, do require confirmation, as can be expected in the coming years.
140: 
141: On the basis of all these findings, we can conclude that there is no danger of whatever
142: kind from the hypothetical production of black holes at the LHC.
143: 
144: Strangelets
145: 
146: Section 5 of the LSAG report deals with the possibility that particles containing strange
147: quark matter, so-called 'strangelets', could be created in Pb-Pb collisions at the LHC and,
148: 
149:                                                                                         4
150: 
151: if stable and negatively charged, could coalesce with normal matter to possibly catalyze
152: conversion of the world into strange matter. This case was already discussed in detail in
153: the RHIC and LSSG reports. There it was argued, as recalled in the LSAG report, that: a)
154: strangelet production at RHIC energies would be less likely than strangelet production in
155: nucleus-nucleus collisions at lower energies such as at the Brookhaven AGS and CERN
156: SPS accelerators; indeed, no trace of strangelet production was found in nearly a decade
157: of Pb-Pb or Au-Au collision experiments performed at high luminosity; b) high energy
158: cosmic rays bombarding the Moon for billions of years have yielded no evidence for
159: events connected with strangelet production.
160: 
161: The results from seven years of running the RHIC program have in fact provided
162: evidence that strangelet production is even less likely at the higher energy of the LHC.
163: They have yielded strong experimental support for the hypothesis that particles are
164: produced from a heat bath at a limiting temperature. The LSAG report shows that, on that
165: basis, strangelet production at the LHC energy, since it involves particles with baryon
166: number, will be less likely than at the lower RHIC energy. Hence, the data from the
167: RHIC program strongly reinforce the arguments collected at the start of the RHIC
168: program: there is no conceivable danger from strangelet production at LHC energy.
169: 
170: Conclusions
171: 
172: The LSAG report considered four specific objects of concern: magnetic monopoles,
173: vacuum bubbles, strangelets, black holes. For vacuum instability and magnetic
174: monopoles, the conclusions of previous reports are confirmed and, in the latter case,
175: reinforced. For strangelets, the conclusions of previous reports are confirmed and
176: reinforced by the analysis of RHIC data. For black holes, the LSAG report goes much
177: beyond previous reports, on the basis of the GM paper. Replacing some highly plausible
178: theoretical concepts of the previous reports with irrefutable observational data on cosmic
179: rays and on astronomical bodies such as the Sun or compact stars, interpreted using
180: firmly established theory, further layers of safety are added to the previously existing
181: ones, excluding any possibility that the highly hypothetical production of black holes at
182: the LHC could create a danger of whatever kind.
183: 
184: To summarize, we fully endorse the conclusions of the LSAG report: there is no basis for
185: any concerns about the consequences of new particles or forms of matter that could
186: possibly be produced at the LHC.
