24:                                                        We analyze macroscopic effects of TeV-scale black holes, such as could possibly
25:                                                    be produced at the LHC, in what is regarded as an extremely hypothetical scenario
26:                                                    in which they are stable and, if trapped inside Earth, begin to accrete matter. We
27:                                                    examine a wide variety of TeV-scale gravity scenarios, basing the resulting accretion
28:                                                    models on first-principles, basic, and well-tested physical laws. These scenarios fall
29:                                                    into two classes, depending on whether accretion could have any macroscopic effect
30:                                                    on the Earth at times shorter than the Sun’s natural lifetime. We argue that cases
31:                                                    with such effect at shorter times than the solar lifetime are ruled out, since in these
32:                                                    scenarios black holes produced by cosmic rays impinging on much denser white dwarfs
33:                                                    and neutron stars would then catalyze their decay on timescales incompatible with
34:                                                    their known lifetimes. We also comment on relevant lifetimes for astronomical objects
35:                                                    that capture primordial black holes. In short, this study finds no basis for concerns that
36:                                                    TeV-scale black holes from the LHC could pose a risk to Earth on time scales shorter
37:                                                    than the Earth’s natural lifetime. Indeed, conservative arguments based on detailed
38:                                                    calculations and the best-available scientific knowledge, including solid astronomical
39:                                                    data, conclude, from multiple perspectives, that there is no risk of any significance
40:                                                    whatsoever from such black holes.

158: 1         Introduction
159: One of the most spectacular manifestations of nature realizing certain extra-dimensional
160: scenarios [1–4] could be the production of microscopic black holes at the LHC [5, 6].3 These
161: are expected to undergo prompt, quasi-thermal, Hawking [9] decay to large multiplicities of
162: elementary particles, leading to very characteristic final states. It has been suggested [10,11],
163: however, that black hole decay via Hawking radiation may not be a universal feature, and
164: could for example depend on the details of the Planck-scale degrees of freedom. While this
165: suggestion is not based on any complete microphysical picture, and furthermore appears
166: contradictory to basic quantum-mechanical principles4 , it does raise a possible question
167: about stability of microscopic black holes that might be produced at the LHC, in TeV-
168: scale gravity scenarios. This in turn has led some to express concern about the fate of their
169: evolution: could their accretion pose any threat to the Earth? This is the question addressed
170: in this paper.
171:     The structure of this work, and a succinct summary of our findings, are outlined here.
172: We begin our work by reviewing what are widely regarded as quite convincing arguments for
173: the robustness of the exceedingly rapid decay of microscopic black holes. We then discuss
174: the arguments asserting that macroscopic consequences of new and unknown particles can
175: be ruled out by the lack of evidence for their effects from production by cosmic rays hitting
176: the surface of the Earth or other astronomical bodies. We argue here that charged black
177: holes will lose enough energy to stop when traversing the Earth or the Sun, via standard
178: electromagnetic processes. Since black holes would be typically produced by the collision of
179: quark pairs, whether in cosmic-ray interactions or at the LHC, they would often be initially
180: charged. To the extent that no mechanism leads to their neutralization, the cosmic-ray
181: based argument for their being harmless is therefore robust. Their neutralization through
182: the Schwinger mechanism proceeds according to quantum principles like those underlying
183: Hawking radiation. There is therefore no concrete framework where neutralization occurs
184: without Hawking decay taking place as well, leading to a likely contradiction in assuming
185: that stable black holes must be neutral. We nonetheless make the hypothesis that this odd
186: situation could occur, and analyze the possible effects of such neutral and stable black holes,
187: beginning with a review of some essential features of gravity and black holes in D > 4
188: dimensions, including both large- and warped-extra-dimensions models.
189:     Next, we develop the formalism to describe the evolution of such black holes trapped in-
190: side the Earth or inside dense objects such as white dwarfs and neutron stars. We introduce
191: and discuss accretion scenarios that apply within nuclear, atomic and macroscopic matter,
192: evaluating the time scales corresponding to various phases in the evolution of a growing
193: black hole. We establish upper and lower limits to the rate at which accretion can take
194: place, building on very basic principles such as conservation laws and classical and quantum
195: dynamics. This is possible since, in order for accretion to become macroscopic, it is necessary
196: that a black hole exerts its influence over distance scales much larger than its event horizon.

205: Such black holes are very small, and their accretion power, if limited to absorbing particles
206: that have impact parameters of the order of the Schwarzschild radius, is typically insufficient
207: to cause macroscopic growth. At large distances the physical processes become independent
208: of the short-distance properties of the black hole, which only acts through its attractive po-
209: tential, and as a mass sink. In this long-distance domain, classical or quantum dynamics are
210: well-tested, making the study of accretion possible, independently of our detailed knowledge
211: or ignorance about the physics inside the black hole.
212:     We investigate two concrete scenarios, for various configurations of the extra dimensions:
213: growth of hypothetical LHC-produced stable black holes within the Earth, and growth of
214: such black holes produced by cosmic rays impinging on dense stars, such as white dwarfs
215: or neutron stars. In both cases, we make conservative assumptions, namely fastest possible
216: growth in Earth, and slowest possible growth in a dense star. The first scenario shows that,
217: if the radius of crossover from higher-dimensional gravity to four-dimensional gravity is less
218: than about 200 Å, the natural lifetime of the solar system is too short to allow significant
219: growth of stable black holes that may be captured inside the Earth. In contrast, in a scenario
220: where the crossover radius exceeds the 200 Å scale, accretion times could be shorter than
221: the solar time scale. In this case, however, examination of the latter dense-star scenario then
222: produces an argument that, given observational data setting the lifetimes of such objects at
223: a billion years or more, such stable black holes cannot in fact exist for a crossover radius
224: greater than ≃ 15Å.
225:    These arguments thus conclude with the exclusion of any relevant consequences for Earth
226: due to the evolution of black holes possibly produced by the LHC.
227:     While the main line of the argument is relatively straightforward, being based on well-
228: established macroscopic properties of matter, several issues require an in-depth study in
229: order to provide a robust basis to our conclusions. When studying accretion, for example,
230: we need to consider the possibility of effects such as the Eddington limit, where radiation
231: emitted during the infall of matter would slow down the accretion via radiative pressure,
232: constraining the accretion rate of a black hole and thus possibly slowing down its growth
233: inside Earth or a dense star. The estimates of the number of black holes produced by cosmic
234: rays need to rely on a solid understanding of the latter’s spectra and composition. Here
235: we therefore consider the worse-case scenarios where, contrary to mounting experimental
236: evidence, the cosmic primaries are dominantly heavy nuclei rather than protons. The loss
237: of acceptance for the highest-energy cosmic ray flux hitting dense stars, due to the intense
238: surface magnetic fields, is also an important element that we analyze. Finally, the slow-down
239: and stopping of relativistic black holes inside dense stars requires a detailed study of energy
240: loss in gravitational scattering and absorption processes.
241:     We note that our bounds are conservative. In particular, at each point where we have
242: encountered an uncertainty, we have replaced it by a conservative or “worst case” assump-
243: tion. For this reason, our bounds can likely be improved through further work removing
244: such uncertainties. Furthermore, we do not exclude that there could be other independent
245: arguments, based for example on astrophysical or cosmological production of microscopic

251:     In outline, the next section summarizes existing arguments (and their extension) against
252: risk from TeV-scale black holes, namely the robustness of quantum black hole decay, and
253: constraints from cosmic rays impinging on objects in the solar system. Section three then
254: summarizes aspects of TeV-scale gravity scenarios and the corresponding black holes. Sec-
255: tion four discusses black hole accretion in Earth. Section five gives an in-depth analysis of
256: the question of stopping of neutral black holes, via gravitational interactions. Section six
257: describes production of black holes by cosmic rays impinging on white dwarfs. Section seven
258: describes the resulting accretion of a white dwarf, and thus derives constraints on hypothet-
259: ical TeV-scale scenarios that otherwise might have been of concern. Section eight describes
260: similar constraints arising from black hole production on, and accretion of, neutron stars. A
261: summary and conclusions appear in section eight.
262:     Many of the technical details are treated in a series of Appendices. Appendix A summa-
263: rizes macroscopic Bondi accretion, which is the canonical framework to deal with the flow
264: of matter into a black hole. For its use in our context, Bondi accretion is generalized here to
265: higher-dimensional gravitational fields. Appendix B discusses the effects of radiation emitted
266: during accretion, and examines the radiative transport conditions relevant to the question of
267: an Eddington limit for the growth of microscopic black holes. In Appendix C we derive the
268: main equations governing the particle scattering and capture in the field of a D-dimensional
269: black hole, and Appendix D proves few facts required by the study of stopping by white
270: dwarfs. Appendix E reviews the formalism for the calculation of black hole production at
271: the LHC and in the collisions of cosmic rays, including the case of high-energy neutrinos.
272: Here we discuss the impact of different assumptions about the composition of the high-energy
273: cosmic rays, we evaluate the production rates under the various assumptions, and establish
274: the most conservative lower limits on such rates. We also discuss the production properties
275: of black holes produced by cosmic rays, to serve as initial conditions for their slow-down in
276: dense stars. In Appendix F we calculate the probability that black holes produced at the

278: general conclusion that trapped black holes would not accrete macroscopically, the veloc-

300: 2.1         Instability of microscopic black holes
301: One of the most profound steps so far towards the yet-incomplete unification of quantum me-
302: chanical and gravitational principles was Hawking’s discovery [9] that black holes evaporate.
303: This provided an important missing link in the pre-existing laws of black hole thermody-
304: namics, by explicitly calculating the temperature that characterizes a radiating black hole.
305:     While Hawking’s result has become nearly universally accepted, it is certainly true that
306: elements of the original derivation of black hole radiance rely on assumptions that are ap-
307: parently not valid. Notable among these is the use of modes of ultra-planckian frequencies
308: at intermediate steps in the derivation. This naturally raises the question of the robustness
309: of the result.
310:     Belief in the robustness of Hawking’s prediction of nearly thermal evaporation has been
311: boosted by arguments for the result which have now been produced from several different
312: directions. These derivations have the virtue of either facing head-on the issue of the trans-
313: planckian modes, or being independent of them, and the basic effect has survived a number
314: of important consistency checks.
315:    One early approach relying on the trace anomaly and avoiding explicit reference to trans-
316: planckian modes was pioneered by Christensen and Fulling [13]. In this approach, the stress
317: tensor describing the Hawking radiation is found by combining the known trace anomaly in
318: two dimensions, and the constraint that the stress tensor be conserved. This approach has
319: been used to give explicit models of evaporating black holes [14], and has also recently been
320: generalized to higher dimensions in [15] and a number of followup works.
321:    Another approach is to modify the short-distance physics to remove the offending trans-
322: planckian modes. One summary of such a prescription appears in [16], where it is argued
323: that despite a different origin for the modes, this approach supports the statement that
324: Hawking radiation persists. This approach is also related to analog gravity models.5 These
325: are models based on condensed matter systems, where a horizon for sound waves occurs,
326: and exhibits a precise analog of the Hawking effect. For example, the abstract of a recent
327: overview talk [18] on the subject of Hawking radiation states “analog models of gravity
328: have given us a clue that despite the shaky derivation, the effect is almost certainly right.”
329: These models illustrate various detailed features of the corresponding radiation in the analog
330: systems.
331:     One of the puzzles of Hawking radiation stems from the fact that it appears to lead to
332: loss of quantum coherence, when followed to the end state of evaporation [19], and this has
333:     5
334:         For a review, see [17].
335: 
336: 
337:                                               7
338: 
339: led to the “black hole information paradox.”6 Many workers feel that the resolution will
340: be that there are subtle corrections to Hawking’s thermal spectrum, that lead to unitary
341: evolution. Thus while very few question that black holes Hawking evaporate, it is clear that
342: there are detailed aspects of the evaporation process that we do not understand.
343:     Indeed, the basic result, that black holes evaporate, appears quite robust from a very
344: general perspective. Nature is quantum-mechanical, and basic quantum-mechanical princi-
345: ples dictate that any allowed decay will occur. Thus, stable (or nearly-stable) objects must
346: be “protected” by conservation laws, examples being baryon number, lepton number, etc.
347: There is no such conserved charge carried by a black hole that is not carried by ordinary
348: matter, if a black hole can be produced in collisions of partons at the LHC. Thus by basic
349: quantum principles such a heavy black hole should decay into light, ordinary matter, and
350: the only question is the time scale. Since such a black hole can have mass at most around
351: ten times the higher-dimensional Planck mass, MD ∼ 1 TeV, the only relevant dimensionful
352: parameter is the corresponding time scale, tD ∼ 1/MD ∼ 10−27 s, and there are no other
353: small dimensionless parameters to suppress decay. Thus, on very general grounds such black
354: holes are expected to be extremely short-lived, as is indeed predicted by the more detailed
355: calculations of Hawking and successors.
356:     Despite these very strong arguments for black hole decay, the possibility of manufacturing
357: microscopic black holes on Earth suggests that one conduct an independent check of their
358: benign nature. For that reason, this paper will test the hypothesis that the statements of
359: this subsection are false, by investigating possible consequences of hypothetical black holes
360: that do not undergo Hawking decay.

363: 2.2       Cosmic ray collisions on Earth
364: Cosmic rays hit Earth with energies known to exceed 1020 eV, corresponding to center-of-
365: mass (CM) energies exceeding 100 TeV. Thus, anything that can be made with Earth-based
366: accelerators up to this energy is already being made by nature. This argument can be used
367: to effectively rule out the existence of particles predicted by some speculative scenarios, or to
368: exclude possible macroscopic consequences of high energy collisions, as discussed for example
369: in [23, 24].
370:     This argument requires however more attention if the large momentum imparted to
371: the produced particles by the cosmic-ray kinematics has an impact on how they evolve
372: after production. Consider the cosmic-ray collision producing the black hole (or any other
373: particle), at the parton level. E1 is the energy of the parton inside the cosmic ray nucleon, and
374: E2 < mp the energy of the parton inside the atmospheric nucleon, where mp is the nucleon
375: mass. To produce a particle of mass M , we need E1 ≥ M 2 /2E2 , and therefore the minimum
376: energy of the produced black hole in the Earth rest frame is given by E1 > M 2 /2mp , or, for

385: the maximum value of M = 14 TeV allowed at the LHC,
386:

389: where pBH is the momentum of the resulting black hole. Any argument that such black

391: proof that, while traversing the Earth, they slow down enough to get trapped by the Earth’s
392: gravitational field. Most such TeV-scale black holes should initially have color or electric
393: charge, since the incident partons (quarks, gluons) are charged. In the usual picture, where
394: Hawking radiation is present, these rapidly discharge through the Schwinger mechanism [25]
395: of particle-antiparticle pair creation in an intense (chromo-)electric field, or through break-
396: ing/absorbing a QCD string. The timescale for the neutralization is proportional to the
397: black hole Schwarzschild radius R. While there is no known example of a consistent mi-
398: crophysics such that Schwinger discharge occurs and Hawking radiation does not, one can
399: point out one difference between them. Specifically, the Schwinger effect can be described in
400: terms of pair production in the gauge field outside the horizon, but Hawking radiation is a
401: trans-horizon effect. In the unlikely event that our understanding of the horizon misses some
402: critical element forbidding black hole decay (an assumption that, in our view, appears to
403: contradict the basic quantum principles outlined above), one might imagine that Schwinger
404: discharge nevertheless takes place. (One could parameterize such a scenario by imposing
405: rather artificial boundary conditions at the horizon.)
406:    Thus, we will consider collisions on Earth in two contexts – those which produce hypo-
407: thetical stable charged black holes, and those which produce hypothetical stable black holes
408: that rapidly neutralize.
409:     Passage of a high-energy charged particle through matter leads to well-understood energy
410: loss [26, 27]. This is due to long-range electromagnetic effects, that have nothing to do with
411: the microphysics associated to the particle itself. Therefore, a muon, or a black hole with
412: the electric charge and the mass of a muon, would be subject to the same energy loss
413: through radiative processes as they move through matter. Since at high energy the radiative
414: properties are mostly determined by the value of the Lorentz γ factor of the charged particle,
415: we describe the energy-loss properties of a singly-charged black hole by rescaling the energy
416: loss of muons to the equivalent γ value. For relativistic velocities, below the threshold
417: for e+ e− pair production in the field of nuclei, the energy loss is described by the Bethe-
418: Bloch equation. The energy loss in this regime depends on the velocity, with a slow growth
419: proportional to log γ. The stopping power for black holes is therefore similar to that of
420: muons, of the order of 2 MeV cm2 /g up to γ ∼ 103 [26, 27]. For the average composition
421: of Earth, this means an energy loss of about 11 MeV/cm. Above γ ∼ 103 (which is the
422: case at the time of production for black holes of mass larger than 1 TeV) pair production,
423: bremstrahlung and nuclear dissociation appear. These grow approximately linearly with
424: energy (or γ), with an energy loss of the order of 60 MeV/cm at γ ∼ 104 , for the average
425: Earth density. With Earth-like densities, the distance scale necessary to slow down from
426: the production energy E ∼ M 2 /mp to γ ∼ 103 is thus of order M/(6keV) cm, or, for
427: M ∼ 14 TeV, more than 104 km, larger than the radius of the Earth. The subsequent slow-
428:

430: 
431: down below γ ∼ 103 takes place with the constant energy loss of 1 GeV/m, corresponding
432: to & 104 km. The stopping distance grows with M , and a more careful estimate shows
433: the Earth provides enough stopping power for black holes with unit electric charge up to a
434: mass of the order of 7 TeV. For larger masses, one can appeal to the stopping power of the
435: Sun. With a core density of about 150 gr/cm3 , over a radius of the order of 0.2 solar radii,
436: namely 1.4 × 105 km, the Sun column density can stop black holes of mass well in excess of
437: 100 TeV. Notice also that our estimates are based on a black hole with unit electric charge.
438: Any minor addition to Q = 1, due for example to accretion of a net charge, would further
439: increase the stopping power. The continued health of the Sun on multi-billion year time
440: scales – during which many such black holes would have been produced and stopped – thus
441: apparently immediately rules out any risk from charged TeV-scale black holes.
442:     The above arguments apply to any elementary charged particle. In particular, they apply
443: to possible magnetic monopoles. The stopping power for monopoles moving through matter
444: is about 100 times larger than that of a charged particle [28], since their magnetic charge
445: is at least 1/α times greater than the electron electric charge. This means that the Earth
446: itself can stop magnetic monopoles of masses much larger than those that can be produced
447: at the LHC.
448:     Let us next turn to the hypothesis that such black holes are stable, but rapidly neutralize
449: via Schwinger production or another mechanism. In this case, the only interactions of a black
450: hole with matter are through its gravitational field. We will give a more careful treatment of
451: such interactions below, but a rough cross-section is the geometric size, σ ∼ 1/TeV2 . Again
452: using the average density of Earth, we find of order one scattering event expected in its
453: transit for this cross section. The maximum momentum loss in a collision with a parton of
454: energy Ep is of size ∼ γ 2 Ep . This is achieved when particles are scattered with significant
455: momentum transfer in the black hole frame, for example in a head-on elastic collision, where
456: the target particle is significantly deflected. Since γ ∼ M/Ep , this would correspond to
457: a loss of a fraction of order 1 of the initial momentum (2.1). However, this is clearly not
458: necessarily typical: due to the short-distance character of the D-dimensional gravitational
459: potential, the impact parameter range for hard elastic scattering is restricted, and typically
460: we shall either have a small-angle scattering, or the target particle can be captured by the
461: black hole, leading to a large inelasticity and to a reduced velocity loss. We shall discuss
462: these issues in more detail in Section 5. The more careful analysis presented there also
463: indicates that the stopping power of the Sun is likewise insufficient.
464:     To summarize, hypothetical stable charged black holes should stop in the Earth for
465: masses up to about 7 TeV, and in the Sun if heavier. The multi-billion year longevity of
466: Earth and Sun apparently provides a good safety guarantee. We have no concrete example
467: of a consistent microphysics such that black holes neutralize via Schwinger discharge but
468: don’t Hawking radiate, but our present state of knowledge of quantum black hole processes
469: doesn’t strictly rule out such a possibility. Thus, we will seek alternative bounds on such
470: a scenario, which will also serve the purpose of improving the stringency of the bounds for
471: charged black holes.

1053: 4.3     Macroscopic accretion, REM & a
1054: 4.3.1   Bondi accretion basics
1055: 
1056: Once the electromagnetic capture radius of the black hole, REM , grows beyond the atomic
1057: radius a , accretion becomes a macroscopic process, with multiple atoms falling in, and with
1058: the gravitational range of the black hole exceeding the mean free path. In this regime, two
1059: effects counter the free fall of matter: the cohesion forces that keep atoms together, and
1060: matter’s finite compressibility. We shall not attempt to provide a simple model to describe
1061: the effect of cohesion forces on a macroscopic scale, due to the varied composition of matter
1062: inside the Earth, with crystalline, semisolid, and liquid phases at various depths. Moreover,
1063: it is possible that once the accretion rate reaches a certain threshold, radiation emitted from
1064: the accreting matter can melt the surrounding material. (This reradiation effect is discussed
1065: in Appendix B, where it is found not to be important until REM 
1066:  1 Å.) Since our aim is
1067: to be conservative and consider the fastest conceivable evolution, we shall neglect the slow
1068: down due to cohesion forces, and treat the inside of the Earth as a non-viscous fluid, free
1069: to fall into the black hole, subject only to the general laws of hydrodynamics, such as the
1070: continuity equation and energy conservation. The compressibility of the medium, which
1071: limits the amount of matter that can be funneled towards the black hole, is accounted for
1072: by macroscopic hydrodynamic properties of the medium, such as its sound speed.

1970: 7     Black hole catalysis of white dwarf decay
1971: The conclusion of the preceding sections is that white dwarfs with solar-size masses, as
1972: well as neutron stars, have sufficient ability to stop a cosmic-ray produced black hole, and
1973: that significant production rates for such black holes can in particular be achieved on white
1974: dwarfs. We will seek bounds from the statement that any stable black holes that could
1975: be produced on Earth will be produced and trapped in cosmic ray collisions with these
1976: astronomical objects. The remaining problem is to examine the subsequent evolution.
1977:     We have in particular argued in section 5.3 that a black hole will be brought to zero
1978: velocity, largely through accretion and scattering, deep within a white dwarf. By this time
1979: the mass of the black hole may grow significantly from its initial value. The surrounding
1980: medium consists of nuclei, for example, carbon and oxygen, embedded in a sea of relativistic
1981: degenerate electrons. A lower bound on the relative velocities between the black hole and
1982: these nuclei is given by the thermal velocity; for temperatures & 107 K, these are at least of
1983: size vT & 3 × 10−4 . At times O(0.6 Gyr) white dwarf cores begin to crystallize, but we will
1984: find accretion to be relevant well before this time.

2142: 7.4     Summary of white dwarf constraints
2143: This and the preceding sections have argued that in the hypothetical TeV-scale gravity
2144: scenarios possibly relevant to LHC, 1) cosmic rays will produce significant numbers of black
2145: holes on white dwarfs of low (. few×105 G) magnetic fields, on time scales short as compared

2438: 9    Summary and conclusions
2439: In this paper we have studied accretion of hypothetical stable TeV-scale black holes in two
2440: primary contexts: the Earth, and compact stars – white dwarfs and neutron stars.
2441:     For Earth, we identified two main evolution domains: that where the black hole’s grav-
2442: itational range of influence is less than the atomic scale, and that where it is greater. An
2443: important distinction occurs depending on where the crossover radius RC to four-dimensional
2444: behavior lies relative to the atomic scale. In particular, if RC is in the subatomic regime,
2445: evolution is four-dimensional at both subatomic and macroscopic scales; this case includes
2446: unwarped scenarios with D ≥ 8. In this case we have argued that this evolution occurs on
2447: times longer than the expected natural solar lifetime, in two different ways: via a microscopic
2448: argument, and via a macroscopic, hydrodynamic argument. In both approaches, we used
2449: conservative assumptions, leading to the largest accretion rates and to fastest evolution. At
2450: the end of the first phase, at times O(1011 ) years, the mass of the black hole is still small,
2451: with a mass of less than a megaton. Such statements extend to more general warped sce-
2452: 
2453: 
2454:                                               50
2455: 
2456: narios, and crossover scales up to ≃ 200Å lead to accretion times longer than the Earth’s
2457: natural lifetime.
2458:    On the other hand, those cases where RC & 15 Å have been treated by deriving bounds
2459: from white dwarfs, and also from neutron stars. In particular, we have argued that in such
2460: scenarios cosmic rays will produce black holes on such astronomical objects, and that these
2461: objects will stop even these very high-momentum black holes. We then studied accretion,
2462: showing that accreting black holes will disrupt such objects on time scales short as compared
2463: to their observed lifetimes. In particular, we found a general relationship (7.15) between
2464: accretion times for Earth and for white dwarfs, which, when combined with white dwarf
2465: ages exceeding 109 years, provides a very strong constraint. Thus, the implication of these
2466: arguments is that such scenarios, where Earth could be disrupted on time scales short as
2467: compared to its natural lifetime, are ruled out.

2522:     We conclude by first summarizing the conditions needed for our bounds to be necessary
2523: to rule out a possible risk. In order for our bounds to have relevance, a sequence of unlikely
2524: things would have to be true. First, TeV scale gravity, with a Planck scale no higher than
2525: a few TeV, would have to be correct, so that black holes can be produced at LHC. Most
2526: workers consider this to be a fascinating possibility, but also a somewhat unlikely possibility.
2527: Second, black hole radiance, which has been deeply studied from a number of theoretical
2528: perspectives, would have to be wrong, and more general quantum mechanical arguments
2529: for black hole instability would have to be wrong. Most workers consider this to be an
2530: exceedingly improbable, if not impossible, scenario. Finally, one would need a mechanism
2531: to shut off the quantum effects responsible for Hawking radiation, but still leave intact
2532: either the quantum effects responsible for Schwinger discharge, or some other neutralization
2533: mechanism that acts to discharge the resulting stable black holes. It is very difficult to
2534: conceive of a consistent physical framework that provides such a mechanism.
2535:     In the event that all these conditions are satisfied, one can turn to the considerations
2536: of this paper to assess the possible impact on Earth. This paper has argued that in order
2537: for such a scenario to have an impact on Earth at time scales short as compared to the
2538: natural lifetime of the solar system, in the five billion year range, the configuration of extra
2539: dimensions would have to be such that gravity doesn’t transition to four-dimensional behav-
2540: ior until around the 200Å scale. This apparently requires additional fine-tuning, reducing
2541: the likelihood even further. But beyond that, this paper has argued that such scenarios are
2542: ruled out by the longevity of known white dwarfs, on billion-year time scales. In such a
2543: scenario, cosmic ray-produced black holes should have catalyzed white dwarf destruction on
2544: significantly shorter time scales.

2548:     Moreover, decay of observed neutron stars would also have been catalyzed, unless both of
2549: two unlikely possibilities are realized, namely that the composition of ultrahigh-energy cosmic
2550: ray primaries is dominantly heavy elements, and ultrahigh energy cosmic ray neutrinos either
2551: are not produced, or have suppressed gravitational interactions with partons. To summarize,
2552: the present study argues for the following additional layers of safety, beyond those that would
2553: have to fail to make this study relevant:
2554: 
2555: 1. Only in scenarios such that the crossover scale to four-dimensional gravity is
2556:     larger than about 200 Å does one have significant accretion at times short
2557:     as compared to the natural lifetime of Earth. This is a-priori unlikely, due
2558:     to the additional fine-tuning required to realize such a TeV-scale gravity
2559:     scenario.
2560: 2. In these scenarios where the bound on black hole accretion time on Earth is
2561:      short as compared to natural time scales, white dwarfs would likewise be
2562:      accreted, on much shorter time scales, in contradiction to observation.
2563: 3. Unless cosmic rays have dominantly a very heavy composition, and moreover
2564:     either the expected neutrino flux doesn’t exist or has unusual gravitational
2565:     couplings to hadronic matter, neutron star decay would likewise be cat-
2566:     alyzed on time scales contradicting observation.
2567: 
2568:    In short, this study finds no basis for concerns that TeV-scale black holes from the LHC
2569: could pose a risk to Earth on time scales shorter than the Earth’s natural lifetime. Indeed,
2570: conservative arguments based on detailed calculations and the best-available scientific knowl-
2571: edge, including solid astronomical data, conclude, from multiple perspectives, that there is
2572: no risk of any significance whatsoever from such black holes.

2593: A      Bondi accretion
2594: Bondi accretion [33, 59] describes the steady flow of continuous matter into a black hole,
2595: assuming spherical symmetry and hydrodynamical conditions. Its basic ingredients are the
2596: continuity equation, guaranteeing a conserved matter flow, energy conservation, which gives
2597: the accretion velocity, and accretion boundary conditions at the surface of the black hole,
2598: which provides the sink for the continuous matter infall. As is well known, many features of
2599: accretion depend on matter properties in the non-relativistic region, far from the black hole
2600: horizon, and so a non-relativistic treatment is warranted. In particular, one may describe
2601: the gravitational dynamics in terms of a general potential φ(r) that becomes strong in the
2602: TeV regime, as discussed in section 3.2.4. Accretion from within a neutron star approaches
2603: the relativistic regime; relativistic corrections, which are typically small, are described for

2753: B       Effects of radiative transport
2754: Acceleration and compression during accretion can cause the infalling medium to radiate.
2755: This raises the possibility of new effects. For example, in the case of accretion on Earth, such
2756: radiant energy could melt the material surrounding the black hole, and thus has potential to
2757: increase the accretion rate. (In our rate bounds for accretion within Earth, we guarantee that
2758: we have accounted for melting by treating the problem as accretion from a fluid.) However,
2759: pressure from the outgoing radiation also has the potential to decrease the accretion rate,
2760: and in particular one should check for the possibility of an Eddington-limited rate.
2761:    The actual process of radiation and its reabsorption is somewhat complicated, but can be
2762: modeled based on simple considerations. In the microscopic regime, one expects radiation
2763: resulting from accelerations of infalling particles. In the macroscopic regime, another rera-
2764: diation mechanism is heating of the material through its compression, resulting in thermal
2765: bremsstrahlung. In general, one can parametrize the reradiation luminosity as
2766:                                                dM

2769: where η < 1 is the fraction of absorbed energy that is reradiated.

3146: This Appendix focuses on the dynamics of a test particle in the background of a D-dimensional
3147: black hole. In the classical context, we consider the trajectory of a test particle in motion
3148: with positive energy with respect to a D-dimensional black hole. The goal is to establish the
3149: features of the particle’s dynamics as a function of the initial energy and impact parameter,
3150: and to define the conditions under which the test particle, in the encounter with the black
3151: hole, is scattered or is absorbed. We shall be interested in applying this discussion to the
3152: study of energy loss, slow-down and stopping of black holes produced by cosmic rays. In
3153: this phase the black hole is still sufficiently small that it only interacts, microscopically, with
3154: the partons inside the nucleon. Since these are in relativistic motion, we need to consider
3155: the case of relativistic test particles. Two kinematical regimes are then potentially relevant:
3156: the classical one, in which the de Broglie wavelength of the test particle is small in compar-
3157: ison to the black hole radius, and the quantum regime, where the black hole itself is small
3158: compared to the probe’s wavelength. In the context of black holes produced by cosmic rays,
3159: the classical regime is relevant early on, when the Lorentz γ factor of the black hole is very
3160: large, and later on, when its size has grown significantly. If we consider first the problem in
3161: the rest frame of the black hole, the momentum of the infalling parton is of order γmp . At

3166: use the known total quantum scattering cross section. Where we need the differential cross

3622: E.3      Black hole production by cosmic neutrinos
3623: High-energy neutrinos have a black hole production rate much bigger than nuclear cosmic
3624: rays of the same energy (see e.g. [7, 93–98]). Two reasons justify this statement. The first
3625: one is that all the energy carried by the neutrino can be used for the production of the
3626: black hole (contrary to protons or nuclei, where the partons carry a small fraction of the
3627: primary energy). The second reason is that the fraction of neutrino interactions that lead to
3628: black hole production is much bigger than the similar fraction of nuclear interactions: most
3629: collisions with a star atmosphere result in generic strong-interaction processes, and the rate
3630: of black hole production is proportional to the ratio of the black hole cross section to the
3631: total inelastic cross section. In the case of neutrinos, black hole production only competes
3632: against the total electroweak cross section [99], which turns out to be of the same size as the
3633: black hole cross section, when neutrino energies exceed 1017 eV.
3634:     While no experimental evidence is as yet available for the existence of such high-energy
3635: neutrinos, any modeling of cosmic ray production and evolution predicts their presence,
3636: with rates that are consistent with the current non-observation. Neutrinos can be produced
3637: directly at the source of the highest energy cosmic rays, where they emerge as decay prod-
3638: ucts of the charged pions produced during the acceleration of the primary charged cosmic
3639: rays [100, 101]. And they also would appear in the decay of pions produced by the colli-
3640: sion of protons with energy above the GZK cutoff with the cosmic microwave background:
3641: γp → ∆+ → nπ + (the so-called cosmogenic neutrino flux [102–104]). The observation of cos-
3642: mic rays above the GZK cutoff, and the assumption that even only a tiny fraction of these are
3643: protons, automatically leads to the presence of neutrinos in the region above 1014 eV. Several
3644: groups have extracted predictions for the cosmogenic neutrino flux, most recently [75, 76]
3645: and [68]. The latter based their analysis on the Auger study of the highest-energy cosmic ray

3905: H       Production on background objects
3906: Given the self-screening behavior resulting from neutron-star magnetic fields, one is natu-
3907: rally led to consider production of neutral black holes on background objects, such as on
3908: a binary companion of the star, or on the interstellar medium. We here outline some such
3909: considerations.
3910: 
3911: 
3912: H.1     On lifetimes and solid angles in X-ray binaries
3913: We would like to understand the range of possible values for the full coverage equivalent
3914: (FCE), defined in (8.1), for neutron star binary systems. A number of such systems have
3915: been well-studied and modeled. Thus, deviations from the FCEs given below would require
3916: substantial revision of our theoretical description of the formation and evolution of these
3917: systems.
3918: 
3919:                                               85
3920: 
3921:    Specifically, neutron stars are often found in binaries tight enough for mass transfer
3922: to occur from the donor to the NS. These X-ray binaries (see [110] for an overview) are
3923: well studied systems, and are also known to exist in other galaxies. The range of possible
3924: donor masses, Md , is large [110], from Md = 0.01M Helium white dwarfs in ultracompact
3925: binaries to 30M stars around accreting X-ray pulsars. The closest possible distance is
3926: when the donor star fills its Roche lobe (e.g. the tidal radius), yielding the largest value for
3927: f = ∆Ω/4π = (1 − cos θ)/2, where [111] tan θ = RRL /a = 0.49[0.6 + q −2/3 ln(1 + q 1/3 )]−1 is
3928: only a function of the mass ratio q = Md /MN S . For Md = 0.01 − 10M (and a fixed NS
3929: mass of MN S = 1.4M ), we find f = 0.002 − 0.06.
3930:      Massive X-ray binaries (see [112] for an overview) have donor masses Md > 5M and
3931: range up to 50M . The longest time that such a system can live as a mass transferring
3932: system (and thereby visible in X-rays) is when the donor always underfills the Roche Lobe,
3933: and the NS accretes some of the stellar wind leaving the companion. Such a system lives for
3934: at most the hydrogen burning main sequence lifetime [113], TM S ≈ 10Gyr(M /M )2.5 of the
3935: massive star, which is < 108 years for Md > 5M . Such a star has f = 0.05, yielding ≈ 5
3936: Myr of FCE. More massive main sequence companions would have slightly larger values of
3937: f , but their much shorter main sequence lifetimes make them less constraining.
3938:    A well-known class is that of NSs accreting from a M ≈ M red giant that result in
3939: millisecond radio pulsars orbiting the remaining He WD core. While the Roche-lobe filling
3940: mass transfer is occurring, f ≈ 0.03, and the lifetime in this phase is set by the nuclear
3941: evolution rate of the red giant. Those that start Roche-lobe filling at orbital periods less
3942: than 10 days can have a mass transfer lifetime of 109 years [110], giving ≈ 30 Myr of FCE.
3943:    The best examples are traditional low-mass X-ray binaries (those with Md < M )
3944: that have shorter 108 − 109 year lifetimes set by the mass transfer rates of Ṁ ≈ 10−8 −
3945: 10−9 M yr−1 . However, at very short orbital periods, the donors become so low in mass,
3946: Md ≈ 0.05M (f = 0.006), as to be brown dwarfs (e.g. SAX J1808.4-3658 [114]) with mass
3947: transfer rates of 10−11 M yr−1 , consistent with that expected from gravitational wave losses.
3948: These are certainly mass-transferring for over a Gyr, giving 6 Myr of FCE
3949:   The lowest mass companions are in ultracompact binaries where a He white dwarf donates
3950: material to a NS at a rate set by gravitational wave losses (see [115] for an overview). The
3951: most constraining of these systems are those at the longest orbital period of 40 minutes,
3952: where T = 109 yr and Md ≈ 0.01M (f = 0.002) and ≈ 2 Myr of FCE.
3953:     In closing, the exposures of known systems are in the range of 2 − 30 Myr of FCE, and
3954: one can be very confident in those scenarios in the 2-6 Myr range [116]. Even the NSs
3955: accreting from red giants that give 30 Myr are relatively robust, and plausibly could be used
3956: in improving bounds.

3959: H.2     Production on the interstellar medium
3960: We study here the possibility that black holes are produced by the collision of cosmic rays
3961: with the interstellar medium (ISM). This is not meant to be a fully robust study, but to
3962: 
3963:                                               86
3964: 
3965: provide an indication of further directions that could be undertaken to produce additional
3966: constraints, largely complementary to ours. We first estimate the column density that cosmic
3967: rays travel through as they reach a star. We consider stars in the disk as embedded in a disk of
3968: ISM of height h and radius L, where reasonable values are given by h ∼ 6×102 light years, the
3969: disk width, and L ∼ 60h. The average ISM density nISM is about 1 proton/cm3 . The average
3970: column density for cosmic rays pointing toward the star is given by nISM (h/2) log(2L/h) ∼
3971: 1.4 × 10−4 protons/(100 mb). This means an interaction probability of about 1.4 × 10−4 for
3972: each nucleon in the cosmic rays. When the cosmic ray is pointing directly toward the star,
3973: and when it produces a black hole, this will end up hitting the star. The star’s magnetic field
3974: has no influence, and therefore both neutron stars and white dwarfs of arbitrary magnetic
3975: field can be considered as targets. The number of black holes is obtained by applying this
3976: reduction factor to the rates calculated for the production of black holes via cosmic rays
3977: directly hitting a star. This suppression is too large to give acceptable rates on neutron
3978: stars. In the case of white dwarfs, and for D = 5, Mmin = 14 TeV and y = 1, this means
3979: a rate of over 30/Myrs for a 10% proton composition (see table 4), and of about 1/Myrs
3980: for 100% Fe (see table 5). Even in the latter, most conservative, case, this means 100 black
3981: holes produced over 100Myrs. Any white dwarf with mass in the range 1–1.2 solar masses,
3982: independently of the magnetic field, will absorb and stop such black holes, which will then
3983: catalyze its decay on time scales short as compared to observed (& Gyr) lifetimes. A white
3984: dwarf like Sirius-B, for example, with a mass of exactly one solar mass and an age of about
3985: 120 Myrs [117], would not have escaped destruction by D = 5, 6 black holes up to minimum
3986: mass 14 TeV, or by D = 7 black holes up to around 10 TeV (see fig. 2).
3987:    Finally, we point out the possible use of massive weakly-interacting dark matter as a
3988: target for black hole production by cosmic rays [118]. While lack of direct experimental
3989: evidence for it makes it insufficient today for our purposes, the expected densities [119] of
3990: about 0.3 GeV/cm3 could provide sufficient to generate large numbers of black holes to be
3991: absorbed by white dwarfs. The much lower γ factor due to production on such a heavy
3992: target would extend the range of capture of even the highest mass black holes to lighter
3993: white dwarfs, and extend the stopping potential to black holes significantly heavier than
3994: those accessible at the LHC.
