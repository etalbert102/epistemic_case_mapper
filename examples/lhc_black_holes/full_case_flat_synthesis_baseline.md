## Flat Synthesis

The LHC black-hole risk case is best understood as a closed technical-risk controversy with a public and legal afterlife. The central safety conclusion is that LHC collisions do not create a catastrophic black-hole risk. The strongest technical support comes from the LSAG review, the SPC review, and the Giddings-Mangano analysis. These sources argue that LHC-like phenomena occur naturally in cosmic-ray collisions, that astronomical bodies have survived such exposure, and that hypothetical dangerous microscopic black holes would require speculative assumptions that are constrained by astrophysical evidence and decay expectations.

The public-facing CERN material states the same conclusion more compactly. CERN explains that the LHC reproduces phenomena that happen naturally, that stars, galaxies, and Earth still exist, and that standard relativity does not allow LHC black-hole production. It also notes that speculative microscopic-black-hole theories predict immediate disintegration. The tiny-black-holes page frames microscopic black holes as a possible signal of extra dimensions rather than as an expected hazard, and says they would decay rapidly if produced.

The technical case is more nuanced than the public summary. The cosmic-ray analogy is not merely that Earth has survived cosmic rays. Low-velocity products from collider collisions could in principle be easier to trap than high-velocity cosmic-ray products. Giddings and Mangano therefore rely heavily on compact astronomical objects, especially white dwarfs and neutron stars, to constrain dangerous scenarios. The SPC review endorses this safety case while distinguishing white-dwarf arguments that apply more directly to LHC energies from neutron-star arguments that bear on broader or higher-energy cases.

The controversy also includes critique and response. Plaga argues that certain metastable microscopic-black-hole scenarios might evade parts of the Giddings-Mangano exclusion, especially if stopping and accretion assumptions differ from the semiclassical treatment. Giddings and Mangano respond that Plaga's power-output argument rests on inconsistent radius-mass assumptions, that Eddington-limited accretion is not established, and that the proposed split between microcanonical behavior and Hawking-radiation assumptions is implausible. This exchange matters because the legal/public question is not just whether the consensus answer is reassuring, but whether residual catastrophic-risk arguments have been represented and answered fairly.

Experimental searches add a later empirical layer. CMS reported no evidence for microscopic black holes in early 7 TeV proton-proton data, excluding production up to certain mass ranges across a set of extra-dimensional models. This does not itself prove the broader safety case, but it updates the public evidence picture by showing that model-specific microscopic-black-hole signatures were not observed in searched ranges.

Johnson's legal analysis reframes the controversy as a problem of institutional decision-making under extreme stakes, expert dependence, jurisdictional barriers, and public trust. He argues that traditional legal tools can strain under claims of planet-ending harm, especially where all experts may have personal or institutional stakes and where direct physical proof may be unavailable before the disputed experiment. His account does not overturn the technical safety conclusion; it highlights why public-risk governance needs an inspectable structure rather than a single reassuring paragraph.

## Likely Erosion Risks In This Baseline

- Public CERN assurances and technical safety arguments are easy to merge, even though they serve different epistemic roles.
- Earth, Sun, white-dwarf, neutron-star, and universe-scale evidence can collapse into one "nature already did it" claim.
- Plaga's critique and GM's response can become a generic dispute rather than a multi-threaded challenge/response structure.
- CMS non-observation can be overread as direct proof of safety rather than model-specific search evidence.
- Johnson's legal/institutional concerns can be treated as social context rather than as a separate decision layer.
