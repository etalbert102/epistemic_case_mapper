## Narrow Question

How should a synthesis preserve the relationship between observational CVD outcome evidence, randomized lipid-marker evidence, guideline framing, and population/context caveats for egg consumption?

## Why This Region Matters

This region demonstrates why ordinary synthesis can be plausible and still erosive. "Eggs are fine in moderation" loses too much; "eggs raise CVD risk" also loses too much. The decision-relevant structure is the relation between endpoints, methods, populations, background diets, replacement foods, and guideline purposes.

## Expected Cruxes

- Whether CVD outcome evidence or lipid-marker RCT evidence should receive more weight for dietary advice.
- Whether "moderate egg consumption" means up to one egg per day or lower typical cohort intake.
- Whether dietary cholesterol effects are separable from saturated fat, animal protein, and replacement foods.
- Whether high-LDL, diabetes, regional dietary patterns, or baseline intake require different guidance.
- Whether short-term LDL changes should be treated as mechanistic warning signals rather than direct health-outcome evidence.

## What Ordinary Synthesis May Flatten

- Observational CVD endpoints versus randomized lipid markers.
- BMJ null/moderation findings versus JAMA positive dose-response findings.
- Eggs as a food versus dietary cholesterol as a nutrient exposure.
- Population caveats: diabetes, high LDL, US/Europe/Asia, typical cohort intake.
- Replacement-food and dietary-pattern context.
- Guideline communications as decision-context synthesis rather than primary evidence.
