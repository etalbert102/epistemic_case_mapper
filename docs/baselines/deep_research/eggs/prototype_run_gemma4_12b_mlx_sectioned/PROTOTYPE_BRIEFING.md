## Decision Brief

For generally healthy adults, egg consumption is considered neutral to potentially beneficial when integrated into a low-saturated fat dietary pattern. Large-scale observational studies indicate that while high intake of dietary cholesterol or eggs may be associated with increased cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk in a dose-response manner, these associations are largely neutralized once for dietary cholesterol consumption. Furthermore, moderate egg consumption (up to one per day) is not associated with CVD risk and may even show an inverse association in certain populations.

**Confidence:** medium

## Decision Implications

- Shift focus from specific egg/cholesterol limits to overall healthy dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean or DASH).
- Prioritize the reduction of saturated fat over the restriction of dietary cholesterol for managing LDL levels.
- Consider moderate egg consumption as a viable protein source for individuals with type 2 diabetes, as high-egg diets have not shown adverse cardiometabolic effects in those populations.

## Evidence Roles

### Main Support

- The PURE study found no significant associations between egg intake and blood lipids, mortality, or major CVD events (Pure Dehghan 2020).
- Saturated fat intake is positively correlated with LDL cholesterol levels, whereas dietary cholesterol was not (Carter Ajcn 2025).
- Consuming 2 eggs daily as part of a low-saturated fat diet lowers LDL concentrations compared with a high-saturated fat diet (Carter Ajcn 2025).
- Moderate egg consumption (up to one per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020).

### Conflicting Evidence

- Egg yolk and meat consumption are associated with cardiovascular harm beyond just cholesterol and saturated fat (Spence Jaha 2021).
- The associations of whole egg consumption with higher all-cause and CVD mortality were largely influenced by cholesterol intake. This challenges the direct association of eggs with CVD risk by identifying cholesterol as the primary driver, whereas Higher consumption of dietary cholesterol or eggs was significantly associated with higher risk of incident CVD and all-cause mortality in a dose-response manner among US adults. states a significant dose-response association for eggs.
- Both claims support the finding that dietary cholesterol, rather than saturated fat or egg consumption itself, is the primary driver of LDL cholesterol and mortality risk.
- One mapped claim (AHA Carson 2020) argues for a shift away from specific dietary cholesterol targets toward healthy patterns due to implementation challenges, while another mapped claim (Zhong Jama 2019) provides evidence that specifically links higher consumption of dietary cholesterol/eggs to increased risk. This represents a tension between clinical feasibility and the underlying epidemiological evidence.

### Scope Limits

- Moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall, but an inverse association was seen in Asian cohorts. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020)
- The study included patients with either a prior cardiovascular event or 2 cardiovascular risk factors, including those with diabetes mellitus. (Prosperity Amhj 2025)
- A high-egg diet (2 eggs/d for 6 d/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) was studied in overweight or obese people with prediabetes or T2D. (Diabegg Ajcn 2015)
- The source document contains PubMed metadata and abstract text, which may not represent the full text of the study. (Eas Ference 2017)
- The study population consisted of over 0.5 million adults aged 30-79 years from diverse survey sites in China. (Qin Heart 2018 Pubmed)

### Method Limits

- The reduction in LDL from eggs may be mitigated by a reduction in less-atherogenic large LDL particles and an increase in more atherogenic small LDL particles (Carter Ajcn 2025).
- A high-egg diet (≥12 eggs/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) did not have adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors in adults with type 2 diabetes. (Diabegg Ajcn 2018)
- Dietary guidance should focus on healthy dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean-style and DASH-style) rather than specific dietary cholesterol targets, as the latter are challenging for clinicians and consumers to implement. (AHA Carson 2020)

## Decision-Relevant Caveats

- The tension between clinical feasibility (moving to dietary patterns) and raw epidemiological data (dose-response for cholesterol) means that while eggs are 'safe' in context, the total cholesterol load remains a measurable risk factor.
- Evidence regarding egg safety is often tied to specific populations (e.g., Asian cohorts or diabetics), which may not perfectly translate to all healthy adults.
