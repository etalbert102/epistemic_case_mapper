## Decision Brief

For generally healthy adults, moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall. However, this finding is contingent on adjusting for dietary cholesterol intake, as the associations between whole egg consumption and mortality are largely influenced by cholesterol rather than the eggs themselves. While some evidence suggests that high-egg diets do not adversely affect cardiometabolic risk factors in specific populations like those with type 2 diabetes, a significant counterposition exists: egg yolk and meat consumption may be associated with cardiovascular harm beyond just cholesterol and saturated fat.

**Confidence:** medium

## Decision Implications

- Dietary guidance should prioritize healthy patterns (e.g., Mediterranean or DASH) over specific cholesterol targets, as the latter are difficult to implement.
- Moderate egg consumption may be associated with lower cardiovascular risk in certain Asian populations.
- The impact of eggs on LDL concentrations may be influenced by a reduction in large LDL particles and an increase in small LDL particles.

## Evidence Roles

### Main Support

- The PURE study included 146,011 individuals from 21 countries and found no significant associations between egg intake and blood lipids, mortality, or major CVD events. (Pure Dehghan 2020)
- Saturated fat intake is positively correlated with LDL cholesterol levels, whereas dietary cholesterol was not. (Carter Ajcn 2025)
- Consuming 2 eggs daily as part of a low-saturated fat diet lowers LDL concentrations compared with consuming a high-saturated fat diet with only 1 egg/wk. (Carter Ajcn 2025)

### Conflicting Evidence

- Egg yolk and meat consumption are associated with cardiovascular harm beyond just cholesterol and saturated fat. (Spence Jaha 2021)
- The associations of whole egg consumption with higher all-cause and CVD mortality were largely influenced by cholesterol intake. This challenges the direct association of eggs with CVD risk by identifying cholesterol as the primary driver, whereas Higher consumption of dietary cholesterol or eggs was significantly associated with higher risk of incident CVD and all-cause mortality in a dose-response manner among US adults. states a significant dose-response association for eggs.
- Both findings support the finding that dietary cholesterol, rather than saturated fat or egg consumption itself, is the primary driver of LDL cholesterol and mortality risk.
- One source-grounded finding (AHA Carson 2020) argues for a shift away from specific dietary cholesterol targets toward healthy patterns due to implementation challenges, while another source-grounded finding (Zhong Jama 2019) provides evidence that specifically links higher consumption of dietary cholesterol/eggs to increased risk. This represents a tension between clinical feasibility and the underlying epidemiological evidence.

### Scope Limits

- Moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall, but an inverse association was seen in Asian cohorts. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020)
- The study included patients with either a prior cardiovascular event or 2 cardiovascular risk factors, including those with diabetes mellitus. (Prosperity Amhj 2025)
- A high-egg diet (2 eggs/d for 6 d/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) was studied in overweight or obese people with prediabetes or T2D. (Diabegg Ajcn 2015)
- The source document contains PubMed metadata and abstract text, which may not represent the full text of the study. (Eas Ference 2017)
- The study population consisted of over 0.5 million adults aged 30-79 years from diverse survey sites in China. (Qin Heart 2018 Pubmed)

### Method Limits

- Dietary guidance should focus on healthy dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean-style and DASH-style) rather than specific dietary cholesterol targets, as the latter are challenging for clinicians and consumers to implement. (AHA Carson 2020)
- The reduction in LDL concentrations from egg consumption may be mitigated by a reduction in less-atherogenic large LDL particles and an increase in more atherogenic small LDL particles. (Carter Ajcn 2025)
- A high-egg diet (≥12 eggs/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) did not have adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors in adults with type 2 diabetes. (Diabegg Ajcn 2018)

## Decision-Relevant Caveats

- The reduction in LDL concentrations from egg consumption may be mitigated by a reduction in less-atherogenic large LDL particles and an increase in more atherogenic small LDL particles.
- Population in other western countries have increased CVD than ones in USA (RR 2.00, 95% CI 1.14 to 3.51 vs 1.13, 95% CI 0.98 to 1.30, P = 0.02 for subgroup difference).
