## Evidence Carrying the Conclusion

Most intervention studies associate high dietary cholesterol intake with elevated total and LDL cholesterol levels, establishing a clear mechanistic link between consumption and surrogate markers. However, the strength of this association varies depending on the specific outcome measured; for instance, while some data focus on lipid profiles, other research assessed all-cause mortality as an outcome to determine broader health impacts. Furthermore, certain US cohorts showed that associations between egg consumption and CVD/mortality were not significant once adjusted for dietary cholesterol, suggesting that the impact of eggs may be confounded by overall dietary patterns.

These findings necessitate a nuanced approach to risk management, particularly for specific populations. For example, individuals with a borderline LDL-c/HDL-c ratio should limit egg consumption due to the observed increase in that ratio. This tension highlights that while general guidelines may permit moderate consumption, those with specific lipid profiles require more tailored restrictions. (See Practical Scope and Exceptions for high-risk subgroup and default population evidence.)

## Practical Scope and Exceptions

- **Default scope:** Egg intake was not associated with cardiovascular disease risk in either participants with or without type 2 diabetes.
- **Comparator effects:** Replacing one egg per day with processed or unprocessed red meat, or full-fat milk, is associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).
- **Exception groups:** Replacing one egg per day with processed or unprocessed red meat, or full-fat milk, is associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).
- **What would change this:** Egg intake was not associated with cardiovascular disease risk in either participants with or without type 2 diabetes.
- **Boundary condition:** The association between egg consumption and CVD/mortality is not statistically significant once the total amount of dietary cholesterol consumed is accounted for.
- **Boundary condition:** The impact of egg consumption on lipid profiles appears to be greater when the intervention period exceeds two months.
- **Boundary condition:** Long-term randomized controlled trials are still required to confirm the actual impact of egg consumption on human health outcomes.
- **Boundary condition:** The study's conclusions are limited to intervention durations of less than one year.
