## Decision Brief

**Decision question:** For generally healthy adults, should eggs be treated as meaningfully harmful, neutral, or beneficial in dietary advice, especially with respect to cardiovascular risk?

Under current conditions, the practical read remains neutral or low-concern and should not be interpreted as a favorable outcome. Key evidence: An updated meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies confirms that an increase of one egg per day is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk. Key caveat: The study's conclusions are limited to intervention durations of less than one year.

**Confidence:** medium

## Practical Read

Under current conditions, the practical read remains neutral or low-concern and should not be interpreted as a favorable outcome. To maintain safety within this boundary, the specific action required is to limit consumption to one egg per day.

This assessment acknowledges the nuance of data heterogeneity and the inherent limitations of current studies.

## Why This Read

The recommendation is based on a lack of significant association in large-scale prospective cohorts, despite substantial heterogeneity.

The evidence details are carried in the evidence and scope sections; this section uses them only to explain the reasoning path.

## Evidence Carrying the Conclusion

Hard outcome data from meta-analyses consistently show no significant association for moderate intake. While most intervention studies associate high dietary cholesterol intake with elevated total and LDL cholesterol levels, these surrogate markers do not always translate to clinical risk. Specifically, studies have not generally supported an association between dietary cholesterol and cardiovascular risk. This distinction is critical because the mechanism of lipid elevation does not inherently equate to a hard-outcome pathology.

However, the evidence landscape includes notable counterweights that necessitate a nuanced interpretation of current guidelines. Early meta-analyses showed a positive association between egg intake and the risk of cardiovascular disease (CVD) and diabetes. Furthermore, one study assessed all-cause mortality as an outcome, providing a broader metric for evaluating long-term impact. These findings highlight a tension in the data: while general associations may be lacking, specific risks can emerge depending on the population and duration of exposure.

This evidence supports current guidelines but requires careful application. For specific details regarding the default population, high-risk subgroups, and additional hard-outcome support, see Practical Scope and Exceptions.

## Practical Scope and Exceptions

- **Default scope:** Egg intake was not associated with cardiovascular disease risk in either participants with or without type 2 diabetes.
- **Comparator effects:** Replacing one egg per day with processed or unprocessed red meat, or full-fat milk, is associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).
