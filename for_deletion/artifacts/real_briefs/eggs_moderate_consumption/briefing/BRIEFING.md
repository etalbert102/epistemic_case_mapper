## Decision Brief

**Decision question:** Given the provided evidence, should generally healthy adults treat moderate egg consumption as acceptable within a healthy diet, and what caveats should change that advice?

Based on the provided source packet, moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is generally acceptable for healthy adults, as it does not appear to increase overall cardiovascular disease risk. However, this decision read carries a degree of uncertainty because the evidence suggests that high consumption may be associated with increased risks in specific populations, such as those with type 2 diabetes.

**Confidence:** medium

## Practical Read

Based on the provided source packet, the following actions are recommended for generally healthy adults:

- **Moderate Consumption:** Generally, consuming up to one egg per day is not associated with an increased risk of cardiovascular disease.
- **Risk Awareness:** Note that egg consumption is associated with a risk of cardiovascular disease, including coronary heart disease and stroke.
- **Specific Populations:** High egg consumption is associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease in individuals with type 2 diabetes.
- **Cancer Risk:** Egg consumption is associated with an increased risk of ovarian cancer.

## Why This Read

This decision read is shaped by the specific scope of the evidence, particularly regarding the study population and dose boundaries. The primary support for this read comes from a study population that included participants from three large US cohorts (NHS, NHS II, and HPFS) WHO were free of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and cancer at baseline (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis). Within this group, moderate consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).

However, the scope of this read is constrained by specific evidence regarding higher intake and different outcomes. Specifically, egg consumption is associated with a risk of cardiovascular disease, including coronary heart disease and stroke (Eggs A 2023). Furthermore, high consumption was associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease in people with type 2 diabetes (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis). Finally, the evidence includes hard-outcome counterevidence indicating that egg consumption is associated with an increased risk of ovarian cancer (Eggs A 2023).

## Evidence Carrying the Conclusion

The evidence mix provides a nuanced view of egg consumption by addressing direct outcomes, mechanisms, and surrogate markers. Regarding hard-outcome support, moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis). However, there is hard-outcome counterevidence indicating that egg consumption is associated with an increased risk of ovarian cancer (Eggs A 2023).

Mechanistic and surrogate evidence further clarifies the physiological impact. Egg consumption significantly increases LDL-c levels and the LDL-c/HDL-c ratio, but it did not effectively increase HDL-c levels (Association Between Egg Consumption And Cholesterol Concentration: A Systematic Review And Meta Analysis Of Randomized Controlled Trials). Consequently, anyone with a high LDL cholesterol level should consider reducing sources of both saturated fat and dietary cholesterol (Here's The Latest On Dietary Cholesterol And How It Fits In With A Healthy Diet).

Finally, the scope of these findings is defined by specific study-design limits. The study population included participants from three large US cohorts (NHS, NHS II, and HPFS) WHO were free of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and cancer at baseline (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis), where moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) was not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).

## Practical Scope and Exceptions

The practical recommendation for egg consumption is nuanced by specific population risks and dietary substitutions. While moderate consumption (up to one egg per day) is not associated with cardiovascular disease risk overall, high egg consumption was associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease in people with type 2 diabetes (Egg Consumption And Risk Of Cardiovascular Disease: Three Large Prospective US Cohort Studies, Systematic Review, And Updated Meta Analysis).

Additionally, the impact of egg consumption may vary depending on what it replaces in a diet.
