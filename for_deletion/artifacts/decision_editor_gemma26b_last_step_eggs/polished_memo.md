# Decision Memo: Dietary Guidance for Egg Consumption

**Decision Question:** For generally healthy adults, should eggs be treated as meaningfully harmful, neutral, or beneficial in dietary advice, especially with respect to cardiovascular risk?

**Bottom Line:** Eggs should be treated as **neutral** for the general population, with a recommended ceiling of one egg per day (or two for older adults with healthy cholesterol). Confidence is medium. While moderate intake does not increase cardiovascular risk, the recommendation is strictly bounded by the presence of Type 2 Diabetes, where higher consumption is associated with increased risk.

### The Case for Neutrality in Healthy Adults
The primary driver for a neutral recommendation is the consensus from major cardiovascular guidelines [AHA 2019], [AHA 2023], which establish that dietary cholesterol does not generally associate with increased cardiovascular risk in healthy populations. This position is supported by high-quality observational evidence showing a hazard ratio of 0.93 (0.82 to 1.05) for moderate intake [evidence decision_writer_item_001] and a relative risk of 0.92 (0.85 to 0.99) [evidence decision_writer_item_002]. Because these findings align with the safety thresholds established by clinical consensus [BMJ 2020], [NNR 2023], there is no evidence-based reason to label eggs as "harmful" for the general public.

### Dose-Dependency and Clinical Boundaries
The recommendation is not an endorsement of unlimited consumption; rather, it is a bounded safety margin. The "neutral" status applies specifically to moderate intake (up to 1 egg/day) [BMJ 2020], [AHA 2023]. We must treat the risk as increasing once this threshold is crossed: for every additional egg per day, the pooled relative risk rises to 1.25 (0.99 to 1.59) [evidence decision_writer_item_003].

Crucially, the scope of the "neutral" advice ends at the boundary of high-risk subgroups. For individuals with Type 2 Diabetes or existing high LDL levels, the evidence shifts from neutral to cautionary, as these populations face a specific increased risk associated with higher egg consumption [BML 2020], [NNR 2023]. In these cases, dietary cholesterol and saturated fat should be restricted to manage arterial plaque [AHA 2023].

### Mechanistic Context and Confounding Factors
While some observational data suggest a link between dietary cholesterol and CVD risk [JAMA 2019], these findings serve as a calibrator rather than an override. For instance, while increased egg consumption can elevate LDL-c concentrations—with a mean difference (MD) of 8.14 mg/dL (95% CI: 4.46 to 11.82) [evidence decision_writer_item_005] and an increase in the LDL-c/HDL-c ratio of MD = 0.14 (95% CI: 0.05 to 0.22) [evidence decision_writer_item_007], and the impact of egg consumption on LDL-c and the LDL-c/HDL-c ratio is more pronounced in interventions lasting longer than 2 months, with an MD = 8.48 mg/dL (LDL-c) [evidence decision_writer_item_008]—these associations often disappear when adjusted for total dietary cholesterol consumption [JAMA 2019]. This suggests that the observed biomarker changes (such as an MD of 8.14 in LDL-c) may be a function of broader dietary patterns rather than eggs in isolation [Li 2020]. Additionally, egg consumption has a negligible or only slight effect on HDL-c levels in healthy individuals, with MD = 1.27; 95% CI: -0.28 to 2.83 [evidence decision_writer_item_009].

### What Would Change the Answer
The recommendation would shift toward "harmful" if clinical trials demonstrated a direct causal link between moderate egg consumption and cardiovascular events in healthy adults, or if the adjusted attributable risk (ARD) of 1.11% [evidence decision_writer_item_016] were shown to be significantly higher and non-contingent on total dietary fat.

**Practical Implication:** Nutrition guidance should focus on adopting Mediterranean or DASH-style dietary patterns rather than isolating egg intake, while ensuring that patients with Type 2 Diabetes are specifically screened for high-frequency egg consumption.
