## Decision Brief

For generally healthy adults, eggs should be treated as neutral to potentially beneficial in a balanced diet, as they are not independently associated with cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk when adjusted for dietary cholesterol and lifestyle factors. While some observational data show a dose-response association between egg consumption and CVD, these effects appear to be driven by total cholesterol intake rather than the egg itself. Furthermore, high-egg diets have shown no adverse cardiometabolic effects in specific populations, such as those with type 2 diabetes.

**Confidence:** medium

## Decision Implications

- Dietary guidelines can shift away from strict individual egg limits toward promoting healthy dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean or DASH).
- Clinical focus should remain on saturated fat and overall dietary quality rather than isolating egg consumption as a primary risk factor.
- Patients with type 2 diabetes may safely include high-egg diets without expected adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors.

## Evidence Roles

### Main Support

- The PURE study found no significant associations between egg intake and blood lipids, mortality, or major CVD events. (Pure Dehghan 2020)
- Consumption of at least one egg per day was not associated with incident cardiovascular disease risk after adjustment for updated lifestyle and dietary factors. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020)
- Saturated fat intake is positively correlated with LDL cholesterol levels, whereas dietary cholesterol was not. (Carter Ajcn 2025)
- A high-egg diet (≥12 eggs/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) did not have adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors in adults with type 2 diabetes. (Diabegg Ajcn 2018)
- There is a dose-response positive association between egg consumption and the risk of cardiovascular diseases (CVD) and diabetes. (Li Atherosclerosis 2013)
- Consuming 2 eggs daily as part of a low-saturated fat diet lowers LDL concentrations compared with consuming a high-saturated fat diet with only 1 egg/wk. (Carter Ajcn 2025)
- Egg yolk and meat consumption are associated with cardiovascular harm beyond just cholesterol and saturated fat. (Spence Jaha 2021)
- Higher consumption of dietary cholesterol or eggs was significantly associated with higher risk of incident CVD and all-cause mortality in a dose-response manner among US adults. (Zhong JAMA 2019)

### Scope Limits

- The associations between egg consumption and incident CVD or all-cause mortality were no longer significant after adjusting for dietary cholesterol consumption. (Zhong JAMA 2019)
- The study included patients with either a prior cardiovascular event or 2 cardiovascular risk factors, including those with diabetes mellitus. (Prosperity Amhj 2025)
- A high-egg diet (2 eggs/d for 6 d/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) was studied in overweight or obese people with prediabetes or T2D. (Diabegg Ajcn 2015)
- The source document contains PubMed metadata and abstract text, which may not represent the full text of the study. (Eas Ference 2017)
- The associations of whole egg consumption with higher all-cause and CVD mortality were largely influenced by cholesterol intake. (Zhuang Plos 2021)
- The study population consisted of over 0.5 million adults aged 30-79 years from diverse survey sites in China. (Qin Heart 2018 Pubmed)
- The study included 29,615 participants with a median follow-up of 17.5 years to assess the associations of dietary cholesterol and egg consumption. (Zhong JAMA 2019)

### Method Limits

- Dietary guidance should focus on healthy dietary patterns rather than specific cholesterol targets, as the latter are challenging for clinicians and consumers to implement. (AHA Carson 2020)

## Decision-Relevant Caveats

- Some studies (Spence Jaha 2021) suggest harm beyond just cholesterol/saturated fat, though this is contested by adjusted results in other large cohorts.
- The quality of some evidence is limited by the availability of only abstract-level data (Eas Ference 2017; Spence Jaha 2021).
