## Decision Brief

For generally healthy adults, the evidence regarding egg consumption and cardiovascular risk is complex and does not support a definitive classification of eggs as harmful or beneficial. While some studies suggest that moderate egg consumption (up to one per day) may be associated with lower cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk in certain populations, such as Chinese middle-aged adults, other research identifies a dose-response positive association between high egg consumption and CVD risk among US adults. Crucially, the strongest counterposition is that high egg consumption was associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease specifically among people with type 2 diabetes (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020 Full). However, some data suggests this association may be driven by total dietary cholesterol rather than eggs alone, and short-term studies in individuals with type 2 diabetes have shown no adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors.

**Confidence:** medium

## Decision Implications

- Dietary guidance should prioritize healthy patterns (e.g., Mediterranean or DASH) over specific cholesterol targets.
- Individuals with impaired kidney function, including the elderly, may face higher risks from egg yolk and meat due to interactions with intestinal microbiome metabolites.
- The impact of eggs may vary significantly by population, such as Asian cohorts showing lower risk compared to US/European cohorts.

## Evidence Roles

### Main Support

- Daily egg consumption (<1 egg) was associated with lower risk of CVD, IHD, MCE, haemorrhagic stroke and ischaemic stroke among Chinese middle-aged adults. (Qin Heart 2018 Full)
- The reduction in LDL concentrations from egg consumption may be mitigated by a reduction in less-atherogenic large LDL particles and an increase in more atherogenic small LDL particles. (Carter Ajcn 2025 Abstract)
- Moderate egg consumption (up to one egg per day) is associated with potentially lower cardiovascular disease risk in Asian populations. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020 Full)
- A high-egg diet (≥12 eggs/wk) compared with a low-egg diet (<2 eggs/wk) did not have adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors in adults with type 2 diabetes. (Diabegg 2018 Abstract)
- The study adjusted for updated lifestyle and dietary factors associated with egg intake, including higher body mass index and consumption of more red meats. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020 Full)

### Conflicting Evidence

- Egg yolk and meat are particularly harmful in people with impaired kidney function, including the elderly, due to the interaction of impaired renal function with toxic metabolites of the intestinal microbiome. (Spence Jaha 2021 Full)
- The increased all-cause, CVD, and cancer mortality associated with whole egg consumption was largely influenced by cholesterol intake. (Zhuang Plos 2021 Full)
- There is a dose-response positive association between egg consumption and the risk of cardiovascular diseases (CVD) and diabetes. (Li Atherosclerosis 2013 Abstract)
- The association between egg consumption and cardiovascular disease risk was found to be lower in Asian cohorts compared to US and European cohorts. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020 Full)
- High egg consumption was associated with a higher risk of cardiovascular disease specifically among people with type 2 diabetes. (Drouin Chartier BMJ 2020 Full)
- The associations between egg consumption and incident CVD or all-cause mortality were no longer significant after adjusting for dietary cholesterol consumption. (Zhong JAMA 2019 Fullish)
- One finding refines the general dose-response association in another finding by specifying that the association with all-cause mortality is stronger in women than in men.

### Scope Limits

- The probability of LDL particle retention and the risk of developing atherosclerosis is low at physiologically relevant levels of LDL cholesterol (approximately 0.5–1.0 mmol/L or 20–40 mg/dL). (Eas Ference 2017 Full)
- The causal effect of LDL on the risk of ASCVD is determined by both the absolute magnitude and the cumulative duration of exposure to LDL-C. (Eas Ference 2017 Full)
- One finding specifies that the association between egg consumption and cardiovascular risk is specifically associated with people with type 2 diabetes, refining the broader population scope of another finding.

### Method Limits

- Dietary guidance should focus on healthy dietary patterns (e.g., Mediterranean-style and DASH) that are inherently relatively low in cholesterol. (AHA Carson 2020 Abstract)
- The study included free-living individuals where the majority (>93%) did not have a history of cardiovascular disease. (Pure Dehghan 2020 Fullish)

## Decision-Relevant Caveats

- The association between egg consumption and CVD may be significantly influenced by total dietary cholesterol intake rather than the eggs themselves.
- Evidence for risk is specifically concentrated in populations with type 2 diabetes, which may not generalize to all healthy adults.

## Audit Trail

- One finding limits the risk of high egg consumption to a specific population (people with type 2 diabetes), whereas another finding asserts a general dose-response relationship for all US adults.
- another finding suggests that the association between eggs and CVD is not significant once adjusted for dietary cholesterol, which challenges the mechanism proposed in One finding that high dietary cholesterol exacerbates homeostasis imbalance in diabetics.
- another finding provides evidence from a 3-month weight-maintenance study showing no adverse effects on cardiometabolic risk factors in adults with T2D, which directly challenges the meta-analysis finding in One finding that higher egg consumption is associated with higher CVD risk in that same population.
- One finding reports a lower risk of CVD in a specific population (Chinese middle-aged adults), which stands in direct tension with the dose-response positive association for higher risk reported in another finding.
